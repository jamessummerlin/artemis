# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 00:00 UTC

## Summary
NASA's Artemis II mission successfully launched on April 1, 2026, from Kennedy Space Center's Launch Complex 39B, sending four astronauts on the first crewed lunar flyby since Apollo 17 in 1972. The Orion spacecraft completed its Translunar Injection (TLI) burn on April 2 and is now on a free-return trajectory toward the Moon, with the lunar flyby expected on approximately April 6 and splashdown off San Diego on April 10. As of Flight Day 3, the crew is preparing for their first orbital correction burn and receiving lunar observation assignments.

## Mission Status
- **Current Phase:** Active flight — translunar coast (Flight Day 3)
- **Target Launch Date:** Launched — April 1, 2026, 6:35 p.m. EDT from LC-39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Notable Changes:** NASA announced in March 2026 a strategic pivot pausing Gateway "in its current form," redirecting resources toward permanent lunar surface infrastructure; Artemis III will now test a lunar lander in Earth orbit in 2027, with astronaut Moon landing pushed to Artemis IV (~2028)

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates
- Artemis II lifted off April 1, 2026 at 6:35 p.m. EDT; all four crew members are in good health and performing mission tasks nominally
- On Flight Day 2 (April 2), the Orion spacecraft completed the Perigee Raise Burn, then successfully executed the ~5 min 50 sec Translunar Injection (TLI) burn at 7:49 p.m. EDT, propelling the crew out of Earth orbit toward the Moon — the first humans to leave Earth orbit since Apollo 17
- The crew completed transition to the Deep Space Network for communications, performed first scheduled rest periods, conducted flywheel exercise sessions, and restored Orion's toilet to normal operations
- On Flight Day 3 (April 3), the crew is preparing for the first Orbital Trajectory Correction (OTC) burn (~8 seconds, scheduled 6:49 p.m. EDT), and Mission Specialist Christina Koch is testing Orion's emergency communications via the Deep Space Network
- The lunar science team in Mission Control is selecting geologic features for the crew to observe during the April 6 lunar flyby, including impact craters, ancient lava flows, and tectonic ridges
- Full mission blog updates available at: https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/

### Technical & Engineering
- All four Orion solar array wings fully deployed post-launch, giving the spacecraft a ~63-foot wingspan and nominal power generation
- Orion mass at TLI burn was ~58,000 lbs; approximately 1,000 lbs of fuel consumed during the 5 min 50 sec firing
- TLI burn placed Orion on a free-return trajectory: lunar flyby at ~4,000–6,000 miles from the surface, then natural return to Earth
- NASA modified the Artemis II reentry trajectory (steeper descent angle) to address Artemis I heat shield charring anomaly — modeling and ground testing confirmed this approach limits char loss within structural and thermal margins
- Orion life support, guidance, navigation, and communications all performing nominally through Flight Day 3
- ESA's European Service Module (ESM) powering, propelling, and sustaining Orion; contributions from 20 main contractors and 100+ European suppliers across 10 countries

### Science & Instruments
- **Lunar Observations:** The crew will conduct geology observations during the April 6 lunar flyby, using handheld cameras with 80–400 mm and 14–24 mm lens configurations; 10 formal lunar science objectives are assigned, targeting craters, lava flows, and surface fractures to inform our understanding of Moon and solar system formation
- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Organ-on-a-chip devices, grown from the crew's own blood-derived bone marrow cells, are flying the same deep-space trajectory as the astronauts and exposed to the same radiation and microgravity; designed to measure biological impacts and inform future crew health protocols
- **CubeSats:** Five shoebox-sized CubeSats were deployed approximately 5 hours after launch into high Earth orbit, contributed by Germany, South Korea, Saudi Arabia, and Argentina:
  - *TACHELES* (Germany): measures space environment effects on electrical components for lunar vehicle technology
  - *K-RadCube* (South Korea): human-tissue dosimeter measuring radiation and biological effects across the Van Allen belts
  - Additional CubeSats from Saudi Arabia and Argentina measuring distinct space environment parameters
- **Science Data Management:** A dedicated Artemis II Science Data Management and Archive system (NASA GSFC, JSC, Ames) is in place for archiving crew observations, imagery, and instrument data

### Crew Updates
- **Commander Reid Wiseman (NASA):** Commanding the mission; becomes among the oldest people to travel beyond low Earth orbit
- **Pilot Victor Glover (NASA):** First person of color to travel beyond low Earth orbit and into the lunar vicinity
- **Mission Specialist Christina Koch (NASA):** First woman to travel beyond low Earth orbit and to the vicinity of the Moon; testing Orion's DSN emergency communications system on Flight Day 3
- **Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA):** First Canadian and first non-U.S. citizen to travel beyond low Earth orbit and to the Moon's vicinity
- All four crew members spoke with ABC News from space on Flight Day 2, describing life aboard Orion and their journey ahead
- The crew is adapting well to the space environment; first rest periods and exercise sessions completed nominally

### International Partners
- **ESA:** The European Service Module is ESA's primary contribution, providing propulsion, power (via solar arrays), thermal control, and life support consumables for the Orion spacecraft; ESA's Space Medicine Team is observing mission operations; ESA declared "Artemis II mission begins" via official press release
- **CSA (Canadian Space Agency):** Jeremy Hansen serves as Mission Specialist; CSA is supporting scientific research during the mission, including coordination with the AVATAR experiment and crew health monitoring
- **JAXA:** Representatives embedded in Mission Evaluation Room to analyze Orion performance alongside NASA/ESA/Airbus/Lockheed Martin specialists
- Over 200 specialists from NASA, ESA, Airbus, and Lockheed Martin are supporting real-time mission analysis

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** Artemis II Lunar Science and Operations
  - **Authors:** NASA Johnson Space Center Science Officer team
  - **Source:** 57th Lunar and Planetary Science Conference (LPSC), The Woodlands, TX, March 16–20, 2026; Abstract #1681
  - **Relevance:** Describes the new "Science Officer" flight controller position managing lunar science and geology objectives; console operational since June 2025

- **Title:** Artemis II Lunar Observations Support Software
  - **Authors:** NASA (multiple centers)
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS), citation 20260001932; also presented at LPSC 2026
  - **Relevance:** Details the Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP) — a structured crew observation guide developed by the Crew Lunar Observations Team — covering the first human cislunar Moon observations in 50+ years

- **Title:** Artemis II Science Data Management and Archive
  - **Authors:** NASA GSFC, JSC, and Ames Research Center teams
  - **Source:** 57th LPSC 2026, Abstract #1583
  - **Relevance:** Describes the data pipeline and archive strategy for Artemis II scientific imagery and instrument outputs

- **Title:** Lunar Silicon Cavity (ultra-stable lunar laser for navigation and timekeeping)
  - **Authors:** Jun Ye et al.
  - **Source:** arXiv preprint, DOI: 10.48550/arxiv.2602.06352 (February 2026)
  - **Relevance:** Proposes an ultra-stable laser in a lunar crater to support future crewed missions navigation and precision timekeeping — directly relevant to Artemis program infrastructure goals

## Upcoming Milestones
- **April 6, 2026:** Lunar flyby — Orion passes ~4,000–6,000 miles from the Moon's surface; crew conducts formal lunar geology observations on the far side and near side
- **April 6, 2026:** Loss of signal / radio blackout during far-side passage, followed by signal reacquisition
- **April 10, 2026:** Expected splashdown in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego, CA; recovery by U.S. Navy San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock
- **Post-mission (2026):** Detailed analysis of Orion heat shield performance, AVATAR organ chip biological data, and crew physiological data to inform Artemis III/IV preparations
- **2027:** Artemis III — lunar lander Earth orbit test (SpaceX Starship HLS or Blue Origin); no crew lunar landing
- **~2028:** Artemis IV — first crewed lunar surface landing under the revised Artemis architecture; Gateway paused
