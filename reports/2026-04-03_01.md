# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 01:00 UTC

## Summary

The Artemis II translunar injection (TLI) burn completed successfully on April 2 at 23:49 UTC (7:49 PM EDT), sending Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen on a free-return trajectory to the Moon — the first humans to leave Earth orbit in over 53 years. Orion is now in Flight Day 3, with crew performing the first outbound trajectory correction burn and a series of medical and systems checkouts during the cislunar coast. A CSA live Q&A with Jeremy Hansen from deep space is scheduled for April 4.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** In-flight — Flight Day 3, cislunar transit; first outbound trajectory correction burn (TCB-1) underway
- **Launched:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35 UTC (6:35 PM EDT), LC-39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **TLI Burn:** Completed April 2, 2026 at ~23:49 UTC — 5 min 55 sec ESM OMS main engine burn; crew committed to free-return cislunar trajectory
- **Current Position:** Cislunar space, coasting toward lunar sphere of influence
- **Lunar Flyby:** April 6, 2026, ~6,513 km from far-side surface; will set human distance record ~252,799 mi from Earth
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10–11, 2026, Pacific Ocean off San Diego
- **Notable Changes:** No schedule changes; all systems nominal post-TLI

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- **TLI burn successful.** NASA confirmed the Orion ESM main engine fired for 5 minutes 55 seconds at ~23:49 UTC on April 2, adding ~900 mph delta-V and placing the crew on a free-return figure-eight trajectory. Post-burn crew quote: *"With that successful TLI, crew's feeling pretty good up here on our way to the moon."* [NASA blog — TLI Go poll](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/02/artemis-ii-flight-day-2-crew-houston-poll-go-for-translunar-injection-burn/)
- **Historic milestone confirmed:** With TLI, Artemis II astronauts became the first humans to leave Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in December 1972 — over 53 years. Victor Glover is the first person of color, Christina Koch the first woman, Jeremy Hansen the first non-U.S. citizen, and Reid Wiseman the oldest person to travel beyond low Earth orbit.
- **Flight Day 3 daily agenda:** Per NASA's published mission agenda, Day 3 activities include TCB-1, CPR demonstrations in microgravity, Orion medical kit checkout, and Deep Space Network emergency comms test. [NASA Daily Agenda](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/nasas-artemis-ii-moon-mission-daily-agenda/)
- **NASA blogs and live coverage** ongoing at [NASA blogs/artemis](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/artemis/) | [CNN live](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/01/science/live-news/artemis-2-nasa-launch) | [Space.com live](https://www.space.com/news/live/artemis-2-nasa-moon-mission-launch-updates-april-2-2026)

### Technical & Engineering

- **TCB-1 (Outbound Trajectory Correction Burn 1):** First of three planned small correction burns to maintain the free-return lunar trajectory. Scheduled for shortly after the crew's midday meal on Flight Day 3. Jeremy Hansen led preparations from the Orion pilot station. Burns on Days 3–4 use smaller Orion reaction control system thrusters rather than the ESM main engine.
- **Free-return trajectory:** No Lunar Orbit Insertion burn required. The mission profile carries Orion ~6,513 km past the Moon's far side (Day 6), using lunar gravity to sling the spacecraft back to Earth for a direct-entry splashdown.
- **Medical kit checkout:** Wiseman and Glover checked out Orion's onboard medical equipment including thermometer, blood pressure monitor, stethoscope, and otoscope — establishing baseline crew health data for the cislunar environment.
- **Deep Space Network (DSN) comms:** Koch tested Orion's emergency communications link via DSN following the transition from near-Earth TDRSS coverage. All DSN links performing nominally.
- **CPR in microgravity:** Glover, Koch, and Hansen demonstrated CPR procedures adapted for weightlessness — a key crew health safety protocol for deep-space operations.
- **SLS program note:** SLS Block 1B and Block 2 development previously canceled (February 26, 2026). Artemis II–V all use Block 1 configuration. Mobile Launcher 2 development also canceled; second-stage solution for Artemis IV+ under assessment. [NASASpaceFlight](https://www.nasaspaceflight.com/2026/03/artemis-course-correction-moonward-details/) | [SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/nasa-revises-plans-for-future-artemis-missions-cancels-upgrades-to-sls/)

### Science & Instruments

- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Organ-on-a-chip devices grown from Artemis II astronauts' own bone marrow cells are flying the identical trajectory, exposed to the same deep-space radiation and microgravity as the crew. This marks the first time AVATAR has operated beyond the Van Allen belts and outside the ISS. Post-mission, single-cell RNA sequencing will measure gene expression changes in thousands of individual cells to quantify radiation effects on blood-cell-producing marrow. [NASA AVATAR](https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/) | [The Planetary Society](https://www.planetary.org/planetary-radio/2026-avatar-and-sungrazer)
- **ARCHER (Artemis Research for Crew Health And Readiness):** Crew health monitoring investigation collecting continuous physiological data during cislunar transit; Christina Koch is primary subject. Data feeds into dosimetry and health models for future long-duration Artemis missions.
- **Radiation shelter test:** Orion's integrated radiation storm shelter is being evaluated during the transit through the cislunar radiation environment, providing data for solar storm emergency protocols on future missions.
- **Radiation dosimetry:** Sensors aboard Orion actively recording radiation flux during the post-TLI coast. Van Allen belt passage during TLI produced expected dosimetry spikes, now resolved as Orion moves deeper into cislunar space. Data being integrated into Artemis III/IV crew exposure models.
- **Lunar observation preparation:** Crew reviewed geological survey targets and remote observation checklists for April 6 flyby. Mission scientists on Earth have pre-selected 10 science objectives and specific lunar surface features for crew observation during closest approach.
- **CubeSats:** Four international 12U CubeSats (DLR TACHELES/Germany, KASA K-RadCube/South Korea, Saudi Space Agency, CONAE ATENEA/Argentina) deployed Flight Day 1 and continuing to transmit science data to their respective ground stations from highly elliptical orbit. [NASA NTRS 20250005942](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250005942)
- **O2O Laser Communications:** MIT Lincoln Laboratory's optical communications terminal confirmed operating on orbit, capable of 260 Mbps downlink for near-real-time 4K video streaming during lunar flyby. [MIT Lincoln Laboratory](https://www.ll.mit.edu/news/lincoln-laboratory-laser-communications-terminal-launches-historic-artemis-ii-moon-mission)

### Crew Updates

- **Commander Reid Wiseman:** Leading crew through Flight Day 3 activities; reported crew in good health and morale high post-TLI. Overseeing Orion systems and coordinating with JSC Mission Control.
- **Pilot Victor Glover:** Monitoring Orion systems post-TLI; participated in CPR demonstration and medical kit checkout. First person of color to travel beyond LEO.
- **Mission Specialist Christina Koch:** Primary subject for ARCHER physiological monitoring; tested DSN emergency communications system. First woman to travel beyond LEO.
- **Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA):** Led TCB-1 preparation on Flight Day 3; preparing for April 4 CSA live Q&A with Canadian media from deep space. First non-U.S. citizen to travel beyond LEO. [CSA Q&A event](https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/events/2026/2026-04-04-artemis-ii-first-q-and-a-jeremy-hansen-live-from-space.asp)
- All four crew in good health; no medical concerns reported.

- **Title:** Optimized Trajectory Correction Burn Placement for the NASA Artemis II Mission

  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20230000223 (AAS 23-062)](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)
  - **Relevance:** TCB methodology directly applicable to Flight Day 3 TCB-1 burn now being executed; describes the three-burn sequence used in Artemis II's outbound leg.

- **Title:** Orion Artemis II Optical Communications Architecture, Implementation and Test Systems
  - **Authors:** NASA/MIT Lincoln Laboratory team
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20250010065](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250010065)
  - **Relevance:** Describes the O2O laser terminal (260 Mbps downlink) now confirmed operational on orbit; relevant ahead of lunar flyby video streaming on Day 6.

- **Title:** Hitching a Ride on History: NASA's Space Launch System Artemis II CubeSats
  - **Authors:** NASA team
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20250005942](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250005942)
  - **Relevance:** Documents four international 12U CubeSats now actively returning science data; includes Argentina ATENEA, South Korea K-RadCube, Germany TACHELES, and Saudi Arabia payloads.

- **Title:** NASA's SLS Super Heavy-Lift Rocket in Process for Artemis II and Beyond
  - **Authors:** NASA MSFC SLS Team
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20250011679](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250011679)
  - **Relevance:** Program context for Block 1 configuration in use; Block 1B/2 cancellation background; RS-25 engine documentation.

- **Title:** MoonAnything: A Vision Benchmark with Large-Scale Lunar Supervised Data
  - **Authors:** Clémentine Grethen, Yuang Shi, Simone Gasparini, et al.
  - **Source:** [arXiv:2604.00682v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00682v1) (submitted ~April 1, 2026)
  - **Relevance:** Learning-based lunar visual perception benchmark supporting autonomous navigation for future crewed Artemis surface missions.

- **Title:** Super-stable laser for lunar navigation and timekeeping
  - **Authors:** (preprint/phys.org summary, February 2026)
  - **Source:** [Phys.org — lunar laser navigation](https://phys.org/news/2026-02-super-stable-laser-moon-future.html)
  - **Relevance:** Proposes placing a laser reference in a permanently-shadowed lunar crater to support precision landing and timekeeping for future Artemis surface missions.
