# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 02:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is nominally underway on Flight Day 3, coasting through cislunar space on a free-return trajectory following the successful Translunar Injection (TLI) burn at 23:49 UTC on April 2. No new NASA blog posts have been published since the 01:00 UTC report; Flight Day 3 activities — including Outbound Trajectory Correction Burn 1 (TCB-1), CPR demonstrations, medical kit checkout, and a Deep Space Network emergency comms test — remain scheduled for the crew's coming workday. The next major milestone is the lunar flyby on April 6.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** In-flight — Flight Day 3, early crew rest/wake period; cislunar coast
- **Launched:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35 UTC (6:35 PM EDT), LC-39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **TLI Burn:** Completed April 2 at ~23:49 UTC — 5 min 49 sec ESM OMS main engine burn; "flawless" per NASA acting associate administrator Lori Glaze
- **Current Position:** Cislunar space, ~100,000+ km from Earth, outbound toward lunar sphere of influence
- **Lunar Flyby:** April 6, 2026, ~6,513 km from far-side surface; will set human distance record ~252,799 mi from Earth
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10–11, 2026, Pacific Ocean off San Diego
- **Notable Changes:** No new anomalies or schedule changes since 01:00 UTC; all systems nominal

### Technical & Engineering

- **TCB-1 (Outbound Trajectory Correction Burn 1):** First of three planned small correction burns. Scheduled for shortly after the crew's midday meal on Flight Day 3 (estimate: ~14:00–16:00 UTC on April 3). Jeremy Hansen is leading preparations from the Orion pilot station. Uses Orion's smaller reaction control system thrusters rather than the ESM main engine.
- **Free-return trajectory:** Orion is on course for a free-return figure-eight profile — no Lunar Orbit Insertion required. Closest lunar approach ~6,513 km above the far side on April 6.
- **ESM-2 nominal:** European Service Module solar arrays continue drawing power; all ESM propulsion and life support systems nominal post-TLI.
- **TLI burn details:** ESM main engine fired for 5 min 49 sec beginning ~23:49 UTC April 2, adding ~900 mph (≈1,450 km/h) delta-V. Two non-critical caution indicators appeared during the burn; crew assessed them as requiring no action.
- **SLS Block 1B cancellation context:** NASA canceled SLS Block 1B and Block 2 development on February 26, 2026. All Artemis II–V missions use Block 1 configuration (8.8 million lbs thrust at liftoff, 322 ft tall). Second-stage architecture for Artemis IV+ remains under assessment.

### Science & Instruments

- **AVATAR organ-on-a-chip:** Personalized bone-marrow organ chips grown from Artemis II crew cells are actively collecting cislunar radiation exposure data within Orion. Now beyond the Van Allen belts for the first time since the AVATAR program began. Post-mission single-cell RNA sequencing will measure gene expression changes from deep-space radiation. [NASA AVATAR](https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/)
- **ARCHER physiological monitoring:** Continuous crew health data being recorded during cislunar transit; Christina Koch is primary subject for this investigation.
- **Radiation dosimetry:** Orion sensors recording particle flux and dosimetry during cislunar coast. Post-TLI dosimetry readings nominal; data will feed Artemis III/IV crew exposure models.
- **O2O Optical Communications Terminal:** MIT Lincoln Laboratory's 260 Mbps laser terminal remains operational; will be tested at maximum range during the April 6 lunar flyby for near-real-time 4K video.
- **CubeSats:** All four international 12U CubeSats (DLR TACHELES/Germany, KASA K-RadCube/South Korea, Saudi Space Agency, CONAE ATENEA/Argentina) deployed on Flight Day 1 continue returning science data. [NASA NTRS 20250005942](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250005942)
- **Lunar observation rehearsal:** Crew scheduled for Flight Day 3 to rehearse the geological observation procedures they will execute during the April 6 lunar flyby; 10 pre-selected science targets near the far-side approach corridor.
