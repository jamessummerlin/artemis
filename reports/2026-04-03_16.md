# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 16:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Day 3 of its ~10-day mission, currently in translunar transit following a successful TLI burn on April 2. New developments since this morning's report include crew live media appearances from space, confirmation of the O2O laser communications system actively operating at up to 260 Mbps downlink, and further details on the Artemis program restructuring: Artemis III has been redesigned as a low Earth orbit commercial lander demonstration, with the crewed lunar landing now designated Artemis IV targeting 2028.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** In-flight — translunar transit, Day 3 post-launch
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC (6:35:12 PM EDT), Launch Complex 39B, KSC
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 11, 2026, Pacific Ocean
- **Notable Changes:** Artemis III program architecture significantly restructured — see Program Milestones section. SLS Block 1B/2 upgrade development formally canceled February 26, 2026.

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- Orion is on a free-return translunar trajectory as of Day 3; all systems nominal.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/mission/artemis-ii/
- NASA press release confirms mission left Earth orbit after the TLI burn on April 2.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasas-artemis-ii-mission-leaves-earth-orbit-for-flight-around-moon/
- Lunar flyby targeted for Monday, April 6, 2026, with closest approach ~4,000–6,000 miles from the lunar surface.
- O2O (Orion Artemis II Optical Communications) system confirmed active; achieving up to 260 Mbps downlink and 20 Mbps uplink via laser link to ground stations at White Sands, NM and Table Mountain, CA — the first operational test of laser communications on a crewed lunar mission.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/technology/space-comms/o2o/
- Deep Space Network and Near Space Network ground assets (tracked via NASA JPL) are providing continuous relay coverage throughout the mission.
  - Source: https://www.jpl.nasa.gov/news/networks-keeping-nasas-artemis-ii-mission-connected/

### Technical & Engineering

**SLS Rocket**
- Vehicle performed nominally through all separation events; no issues reported post-launch.
- SLS Block 1 remains the only SLS configuration in the program's future following the February 26, 2026 cancellation of Block 1B and Block 2 upgrades.

**Orion Spacecraft ("Integrity")**
- All four solar array wings remain deployed and locked; power generation nominal.
- Proximity operations demonstration on April 1 (70 minutes of manual flight by Pilot Glover) logged performance data exceeding simulator predictions.
- TLI burn on April 2: 5 minutes 50 seconds, service module main engine, ~1,000 lbs of fuel expended from 58,000 lb vehicle, successfully placed Orion on free-return lunar trajectory.
- Minor email system issue noted but did not affect operations; resolved.
- Universal Waste Management System (toilet) urine collection fan jam from Day 1 resolved by crew and ground teams. All systems functioning normally.

**Heat Shield**
- No new in-flight data; reentry monitoring of the modified steeper trajectory approach will be a key post-mission evaluation point.
- Pre-flight context: NASA chose a steeper direct-reentry profile (eliminating the skip maneuver) to reduce time in peak thermal environment, in response to the 100+ anomalous char-loss zones found on Artemis I's Avcoat heat shield.

**Optical Communications (O2O)**
- The Orion Artemis II Optical Communications payload uses a 4-inch telescope, dual-axis gimbals, a modem, and a flight computer.
- 260 Mbps downlink / 20 Mbps uplink represents a significant advancement over legacy RF deep space communications.
- Ground terminals: White Sands, NM and Table Mountain, CA.
  - Sources: https://www.nasa.gov/technology/space-comms/o2o/ | https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250010065

**Trajectory**
- Free-return profile confirmed: no lunar orbit insertion. Closest lunar approach ~April 6, then free-return transit to Earth.
- Crew will attempt to set a new crewed distance record (~252,000 miles from Earth), surpassing Apollo 13's ~248,655 miles.

### Science & Instruments

**O2O Laser Communications (in-flight)**
- Actively operational as of Day 2/3, transmitting at up to 260 Mbps. First real operational demonstration of laser comms from a crewed mission at lunar distances.

**Crew Health Experiments**
- AVATAR (organ-on-a-chip radiation and microgravity study) and ARCHeR (wearable health monitoring wristband) are both active.

**Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP)**
- The Artemis II Lunar Science Team developed and deployed a Lunar Targeting Plan software system providing the crew with real-time structured observation guidance for the lunar flyby, identifying scientifically high-priority targets on the near and far sides.
  - Source: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260001932

**CubeSats (Deployed April 1, ~5 hours post-launch)**
- K-RadCube (South Korea / KASA / KASI): tissue-mimicking dosimetry measuring radiation bioeffects across Van Allen Belts. Confirmed operational.
- Space Weather CubeSat-1 (Saudi Arabia / Saudi Space Agency): solar wind, X-ray, energetic particle, and magnetic field monitoring.
- TACHELES (Germany / DLR): electrical component testing for future lunar logistics vehicles.
- ATENEA (Argentina / CONAE): radiation shielding, orbital design, GPS testing, long-range comms demonstration.
- All four are deployed and in independent operations.

**Deep Space Radiation Monitoring**
- Artemis I BioSentinel data and ongoing radiation environment measurements are feeding into Artemis III crew storm-shelter protocols and EVA planning.
  - Source: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250002757

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** Hitching a Ride on History: NASA's SLS Artemis II CubeSats
  - **Authors:** NASA/GSFC team (multiple)
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS), 2025
  - **URL:** https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250005942
  - **Relevance:** Technical documentation of all four international CubeSat payloads deployed from the SLS stage adapter on Artemis II; covers TACHELES, ATENEA, K-RadCube, and Space Weather CubeSat-1 objectives and designs.

- **Title:** Orion Artemis II Optical Communications Architecture
  - **Authors:** NASA/GSFC O2O team
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS), 2025
  - **URL:** https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250010065
  - **Relevance:** Technical architecture of the O2O laser communications terminal currently operating aboard Orion; details 260 Mbps downlink design, telescope and gimbal specs, and ground terminal configuration.

- **Title:** Artemis II Lunar Observations Support Software
  - **Authors:** Artemis II Lunar Science Team
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS), 2026
  - **URL:** https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260001932
  - **Relevance:** Documents the Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP) software developed to give the crew real-time observation targeting during the lunar flyby; describes the science decision support system architecture.

- **Title:** Deep Space Radiation Measurements for the Artemis Program
  - **Authors:** NASA radiation sciences team
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS), 2025
  - **URL:** https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250002757
  - **Relevance:** Radiation environment characterization derived from Artemis I mission instruments and BioSentinel data; directly informs Artemis II and Artemis III crew safety protocols.

- **Title:** Artemis-enabled Stellar Imager (AeSI): NASA NIAC Phase I Final Report
  - **Authors:** Carpenter et al.
  - **Source:** arXiv:2503.02105 (submitted March 2025)
  - **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.02105
  - **Relevance:** Concept study for a lunar surface UV/optical interferometer leveraging Artemis infrastructure; uses Artemis II-demonstrated capabilities as a programmatic baseline for future lunar science.

- **Title:** Evaluating Artemis III Landing Sites: A Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis
  - **Authors:** Multiple (planetary science community)
  - **Source:** arXiv:2406.19863 (2024)
  - **URL:** https://arxiv.org/html/2406.19863v2
  - **Relevance:** Systematic evaluation of 1,247 candidate lunar south pole landing sites for Artemis III (now designated Artemis IV); informs mission planning for the first crewed lunar landing.

- **Title:** Artemis II is NASA's last moon mission without Silicon Valley
  - **Source:** TechCrunch, April 2, 2026
  - **URL:** https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/02/artemis-ii-is-nasas-last-moon-mission-without-silicon-valley/
  - **Relevance:** Analysis piece framing Artemis II as the programmatic inflection point after which commercial landers (SpaceX HLS, Blue Origin) and commercial crew elements dominate the lunar architecture.

## Upcoming Milestones

- **~April 6, 2026 (~1:45 PM EDT):** Lunar closest approach (~4,000–6,000 miles from surface); crew photographs lunar far side; potential new crewed distance record (~252,000 miles from Earth, exceeding Apollo 13's ~248,655 miles).
- **~April 11, 2026:** Planned splashdown in the Pacific Ocean; U.S. Navy recovery operations.
- **Post-mission (April/May 2026):** Post-flight inspection of Orion heat shield; results critical to validating the modified reentry trajectory approach before Artemis IV planning.
- **Mid-2027:** Artemis III — redesigned as a low Earth orbit demonstration mission to test commercial Human Landing System vehicles (SpaceX Starship HLS and/or Blue Origin Blue Moon) and Axiom Space EVA suits. No crewed lunar landing in this mission.
  - Source: https://spacepolicyonline.com/news/more-delays-for-artemis-artemis-ii-slips-to-april-2026-artemis-iii-to-mid-2027/
- **~2028:** Artemis IV — first crewed lunar landing mission (formerly designated Artemis III).
- **Canceled:** Lunar Gateway space station program (canceled March 2026). SLS Block 1B and Block 2 development (canceled February 26, 2026).
- **SpaceX Starship simplification study:** SpaceX is formally evaluating a simplified Starship-based Artemis lunar landing concept of operations to improve safety margins and accelerate schedule.
  - Source: https://www.space.com/space-exploration/artemis/spacex-looking-into-simplified-starship-artemis-3-mission-to-get-astronauts-to-the-moon-faster
