# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-02 02:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is now in flight, having successfully launched on April 1, 2026 at 6:35 PM EDT from Kennedy Space Center's Launch Complex 39B — the first crewed mission beyond low Earth orbit in over 50 years. On Day 1, the crew completed a proximity operations demonstration with the ICPS upper stage, and the Trans-Lunar Injection (TLI) burn is scheduled for April 2 to set Orion on course for a free-return lunar flyby on April 6. Separately, the Lunar Gateway program has been cancelled and Artemis III has been restructured from a Moon landing to an orbital lander test mission in 2027.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** In-flight — Day 2 of ~10-day mission; TLI burn imminent
- **Target Launch Date:** Launched April 1, 2026 at 6:35 PM EDT (22:35 UTC)
- **Notable Changes:** Gateway programme cancelled (March 2026); Artemis III redesigned as LEO lander test (no lunar landing); Artemis IV now carries first crewed Moon landing target (early 2028)

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- **Launch Confirmed:** SLS Block 1 with Orion spacecraft *Integrity* lifted off from LC-39B at 6:35 PM EDT on April 1, generating ~8.8 million pounds of thrust at liftoff. [Liftoff press release](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/liftoff-nasa-launches-astronauts-on-historic-artemis-moon-mission/)
- **Solar Array Deployment:** All four Orion solar array wings (SAWs) fully deployed shortly after launch, confirming power generation. [NASA Artemis blog](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/artemis/)
- **Perigee Raise Maneuver Complete:** The ICPS RL10 engine fired to raise the lowest point of Orion's initial Earth orbit. [NASA blog post](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/01/artemis-ii-flight-update-perigee-raise-maneuver-complete-nasa-to-hold-press-conference/)
- **Apogee Raise Burn Complete:** A subsequent ICPS burn raised the highest point of the orbit, placing Orion in a ~23.5-hour checkout orbit. [NASA blog post](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/01/artemis-ii-flight-update-apogee-raise-burn-complete-crew-looks-ahead-to-proximity-operations/)
- **Intuitive Machines CLPS Award:** On March 27, NASA awarded Intuitive Machines $180.4 million to deliver NASA-funded science and technology payloads to the lunar surface under the CLPS program. [NASA press release](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/clps/nasa-selects-intuitive-machines-to-deliver-artemis-science-tech-to-moon/)
- **Mission Coverage:** Daily mission status briefings are scheduled from JSC beginning April 2, with the exception of April 6 (lunar flyby). [NASA coverage page](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasa-sets-coverage-for-artemis-ii-moon-mission/)

### Technical & Engineering

- **Proximity Operations Demonstration (Flight Day 1):** Approximately 3.5 hours post-launch, Pilot Victor Glover took manual control of Orion after separation from the ICPS. Over a ~70-minute demonstration:
  - Orion performed an automated backflip to face the ICPS
  - At ~300 feet from the ICPS, Orion halted relative motion
  - At ~30 feet, fine handling qualities were evaluated using translational and rotational hand controllers
  - Small maneuvers near the ICPS were executed via ESM reaction control system (RCS) thrusters
  - Orion then performed an automated departure burn; the ICPS subsequently deorbited over the Pacific Ocean
  - Docking camera data gathered will inform future navigation in the lunar environment (no GPS)
- **Trans-Lunar Injection Burn (Planned April 2):** The ESM orbital maneuvering system engine (~6,000 lb thrust) will fire for approximately 6 minutes on April 2 to send Orion on its free-return figure-eight trajectory. This is the last major engine firing of the mission. Mission Specialist Christina Koch is preparing the burn sequence.
- **European Service Module:** ESA's ESM — built by Airbus, integrating contributions from over 20 companies across 10 ESA member states — is performing nominally. The ESM provides propulsion (1 main engine, 8 auxiliary engines, 24 RCS thrusters), power (four 7-metre solar arrays), and life support. ESA engineers are monitoring from ESTEC (Netherlands) and ESOC (Germany) as well as JSC Houston. [ESA ESM overview](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Orion/Artemis_II)
- **Trajectory Profile:** Orion is on a free-return lunar trajectory; no Lunar Orbit Insertion burn is planned. Closest lunar approach is ~6,513 km from the far-side surface. The crew is expected to exceed the Apollo 13 farthest-from-Earth record (~248,655 miles / 400,171 km). [NTRS trajectory paper](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)

### Science & Instruments

- **Mission Objectives:** Artemis II is primarily a systems demonstration flight — validating Orion's life support, navigation, communications, and deep-space operations with crew aboard. No science instruments land on the Moon.
- **Crew Observations:** During the April 6 lunar flyby, the astronauts will photograph and visually observe portions of the lunar far side, the first humans to directly observe some of those regions.
- **Medical & Human Research:** The crew will participate in medical monitoring and human factors experiments throughout the mission, supporting data collection on deep-space physiological effects.
- **Proximity Ops Data:** Navigation positioning data from the docking camera during proximity operations will feed into future Artemis rendezvous algorithms.

### Crew Updates

- **Commander Reid Wiseman** — overseeing spacecraft operations and the proximity ops demonstration.
- **Pilot Victor Glover** — manually piloted Orion during proximity operations on Flight Day 1; exclaimed on live broadcast: *"I see it. Look at that, woohoo! I see the ICPS and the moon in the field of view."* Will become the first person of color to travel to the Moon's vicinity.
- **Mission Specialist Christina Koch** — leading TLI burn preparations on April 2. First woman to travel to the lunar vicinity.
- **Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA)** — first Canadian and first non-U.S. citizen to fly to the Moon's vicinity; on his first spaceflight. Three live space-to-Earth connections scheduled (mission days 4, 5, and 8). [CSA mission page](https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/missions/artemis-ii/)

### International Partners

- **ESA:** The European Service Module is the propulsion and power backbone of Orion; performing nominally in-flight. ESA engineers on console at ESTEC, ESOC, and JSC. Third and fourth ESMs already delivered and undergoing testing for Artemis III and IV. [ESA Artemis II page](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Orion/Artemis_II); [ESA in-flight update](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Artemis_II_mission_begins)
- **CSA:** Jeremy Hansen is on-orbit as the first Canadian on a lunar mission. CSA's future contribution includes Canadarm3 for Gateway (programme now restructured to surface focus). CSA astronaut Jenni Gibbons serves as Hansen's backup. [CSA Artemis II page](https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/missions/artemis-ii/)

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** Optimized Trajectory Correction Burn Placement for the NASA Artemis II Mission
  - **Authors:** David Woffinden, Randy Eckman, Shane Robinson (NASA JSC)
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS, NTRS ID 20230000223 (AAS 23-062)](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)
  - **Relevance:** Describes the robust trajectory optimization methodology used to determine the placement of trajectory correction burns (TCBs) during the Artemis II free-return mission, accounting for GN&C performance, navigation uncertainty, crew schedule, and thruster selection. Directly applicable to the in-flight correction burns being executed now.

- **Title:** MoonAnything: A Vision Benchmark with Large-Scale Lunar Supervised Data
  - **Authors:** Clémentine Grethen, Yuang Shi, Simone Gasparini, et al.
  - **Source:** [arXiv:2604.00682v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00682v1)
  - **Relevance:** Addresses learning-based perception for lunar surface navigation — directly supports future Artemis surface missions and autonomous systems. Coincidentally published days before the Artemis II launch.
