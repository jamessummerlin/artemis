# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-14 14:00 UTC

## Summary

Post-mission engineering reviews have confirmed two notable Orion technical anomalies: a helium pressurant valve leak in the European Service Module that grew "an order of magnitude" beyond preflight measurements — likely requiring extensive redesign before Artemis IV — and a wastewater vent-line clog that periodically made the toilet unusable. NASA has also released its FY 2027 budget request ($18.8B total; $8.5B Exploration), committing to a first crewed lunar landing in 2028 with Artemis IV, while Artemis III remains on track as a mid-2027 LEO lander-docking test.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Post-mission — crew reconditioning, medical evaluations, and science debriefs at JSC; engineering anomaly reviews underway
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC; Splashdown: April 10, 2026 at ~00:07 UTC
- **Mission Duration:** ~9 days, 1 hour, 31 minutes
- **Notable Changes:**
  - All primary mission objectives declared achieved; mission a full success
  - ESM helium valve leak confirmed at ~10× preflight rate; design fix required before Artemis IV lunar-orbit missions
  - Wastewater vent-line clog documented as quality-of-life anomaly; toilet periodically unusable mid-mission
  - FY 2027 NASA budget request released April 2026: $18.8B total, $8.5B Exploration/Artemis
  - Media credentialing deadline for April 16 postflight news conference: **today, April 14, 5 p.m. CDT**

### Technical & Engineering

- **Helium pressurant valve leak — "order of magnitude" worse:** Post-splashdown NASA engineering review confirmed that the Orion/ESM propellant tank pressurant valve system experienced a helium leak during flight that was approximately 10× the rate observed during ground testing. NASA officials stated the system "remained within acceptable limits" for the Artemis II mission profile (no sustained lunar orbit required) and the leak did not endanger the crew. However, the anomaly will likely require an "extensive redesign" of the valve system before Artemis IV, the first crewed lunar-orbit-and-landing mission (~2028). The current valve design may still be used for Artemis III, which will be a LEO docking test requiring far less propulsion demand.
  - [Artemis II Is About to Splash Down. There's a Helium Leak That's 10 Times Worse Than Expected. — 19FortyFive](https://www.19fortyfive.com/2026/04/artemis-ii-is-about-to-splash-down-theres-a-helium-leak-thats-10-times-worse-than-expected-nasa-says-its-fine/)
  - [Orion Helium Leak Safe but Forces Valve Redesign — Technology.org](https://www.technology.org/2026/04/10/orions-helium-leak-wont-endanger-artemis-ii-crew-but-nasa-needs-new-valves/)
  - [NASA managers outline Artemis 2 reentry and address propulsion issue — SpaceQ Media](https://spaceq.ca/nasa-managers-outline-artemis-2-reentry-and-address-propulsion-issue-ahead-of-splashdown/)
- **Toilet/wastewater vent-line anomaly documented:** A wastewater vent line became clogged during the mission, limiting the ability to empty the urine storage tank. When the tank filled, the toilet became temporarily unusable; crew used plastic bags as a backup. NASA and Lockheed Martin engineers have flagged this as a system requiring fix before future long-duration deep-space flights. Flight Day 2 troubleshooting blog documented early resolution of the initial toilet issue; the vent-line clog recurred later in the mission.
  - [Artemis II Flight Update: Crew and Ground Teams Successfully Troubleshoot Orion's Toilet — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/02/artemis-ii-flight-update-crew-and-ground-teams-successfully-troubleshoot-orions-toilet/)
  - [What Is Going On With the Artemis 2 Toilet? — Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/what-is-going-on-with-the-artemis-2-toilet-2000743998)
  - [Orion splashes down to successfully end Artemis 2 mission — SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/orion-splashes-down-to-successfully-end-artemis-2-mission/)
- **ESM propulsion precision — two burns cancelled:** ESA confirmed that the European Service Module main engine performed the translunar injection burn so precisely that two of the planned trajectory correction burns were not required. The ESM powered the spacecraft over 1 million km through deep space, supplied life support consumables, and all four solar arrays performed nominally throughout the 10-day mission.
  - [ESA — Splashdown for Artemis II](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Artemis_II_splashdown)
  - [ESA — European Service Module engines powering Artemis II](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/European_Service_Module_engines_powering_Artemis_II)
- **Heat shield assessment ongoing:** Post-flight inspections of the Orion AVCOAT heat shield continue at JSC. No public anomaly statements issued since splashdown; engineers are comparing performance data against the modified loft-reentry trajectory profile.
  - [NASA Shares Orion Heat Shield Findings, Updates Artemis Moon Missions — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-shares-orion-heat-shield-findings-updates-artemis-moon-missions/)
- **'Perfect bullseye splashdown' assessment:** Mission managers and analysts are characterizing the reentry and recovery as textbook. Aerospace America published a post-mission recap.
  - ['Perfect bullseye splashdown' caps Artemis II lunar mission — Aerospace America](https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/perfect-bullseye-splashdown-caps-artemis-ii-lunar-mission/)

### Science & Instruments

- **5 mission takeaways (CNN, April 11):** CNN's post-mission analysis highlights five key lessons: (1) Artemis proves humans can safely fly to the Moon; (2) the heat shield needs further study; (3) the service module valve system requires redesign; (4) the toilet/life-support system needs improvement; and (5) the mission generated unprecedented far-side imagery that advances lunar science.
  - [5 takeaways from the Artemis II mission — CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/11/science/moon-mission-takeaways-artemis-2)
- **Lunar science data processing underway:** CLO (Crew Lunar Observations) team at JSC continues analysis of the 7,000+ images captured during the April 6 flyby, including the first human-eye observations of Orientale Basin and the lunar far side.
- **Radiation/SEP dosimetry data:** No significant solar energetic particle events occurred during the mission; crew radiation dose data is being processed and will inform deep-space mission planning.
- **AVATAR and ARCHeR bio-sample analysis:** Human Research Program investigations continue at JSC with crew biological samples and health monitoring data collected during the flight.
