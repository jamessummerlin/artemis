# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 07:00 UTC

## Summary
Artemis II is approximately 33 hours into its ~10-day mission, with Orion "Integrity" and its four-person crew well into translunar transit following a successful TLI burn late on April 2. Flight Day 3 begins with the crew healthy and all primary spacecraft systems nominal after a toilet fan anomaly was resolved on Flight Day 1. Lunar closest approach remains on track for ~April 6. In broader Artemis program news, Artemis III has been restructured and will **no longer land on the Moon** — rendezvous/docking tests in low Earth orbit are now the mission objective, with the first crewed lunar surface landing pushed to Artemis IV (~2028).

## Mission Status
- **Current Phase:** In-flight — translunar transit, Flight Day 3 (April 3, 2026)
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC (6:35 p.m. EDT) from Launch Complex 39B, KSC
- **Elapsed Mission Time:** ~33 hours
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10, 2026 (~8:06 p.m. EDT), Pacific Ocean off San Diego; U.S. Navy recovery vessel (San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock)
- **Notable Changes:** No new trajectory or schedule changes. Mission proceeding nominally following pre-launch delays caused by a liquid hydrogen leak (February wet dress rehearsal) and a helium flow issue that triggered a VAB rollback in March.

### Technical & Engineering

**Orion Spacecraft ("Integrity")**
- Toilet anomaly details: Immediately after launch, a fault in the toilet's fan controller disabled the urine collection function. For approximately six hours, at least one crew member used a NASA flight-approved contingency bag. Crew and Mission Control isolated the fault and restored full toilet functionality before end of Flight Day 1.
  - Source: https://theprint.in/science/artemis-ii-and-its-toilet-troubles-it-failed-after-takeoff/2894861/
- All four solar array wings confirmed generating nominal power throughout transit.
- Orion's ESM main engine fired for ~5 minutes 50 seconds on April 2 (~23:49 UTC) for TLI, placing the spacecraft on a free-return lunar trajectory.

**SLS Rocket (post-separation)**
- SLS Block 1 configuration: 322 ft tall; 5.7 million lb liftoff mass; 8.8 million lb thrust at liftoff.
- Core stage (4 RS-25 engines) and ICPS separated nominally. New-production RS-25 engines were certified in 2024 by NASA and L3Harris Technologies (NTRS 20250011679).
- Two solid rocket boosters (177 ft each) generated 3.6 million lb thrust; jettisoned ~2 minutes post-launch.
- All four international CubeSats were deployed from the SLS stage adapter approximately 5 hours after launch.

**Heat Shield**
- No new heat shield status updates. The pre-launch controversy centered on Artemis I's post-flight char loss findings; NASA's mitigations (redesigned Avcoat porosity, direct reentry trajectory) were reviewed and approved at the March 12, 2026 Flight Readiness Review.
- Post-mission heat shield inspection will be a critical data point for Artemis III planning.

**Trajectory**
- Free-return trajectory (same type used by Apollo 13): no course correction burns publicly reported; trajectory nominal.
- Closest lunar approach: crew passes ~4,700 miles beyond the far side of the Moon on ~April 6, with a ~6-hour observation window.
- Distance record attempt: ~252,021 statute miles from Earth, exceeding Apollo 13's record by ~3,366 miles, also targeted for ~April 6.
- Reentry speed: ~25,000 mph (~40,000 km/h) into Earth's atmosphere.

**Ground Systems**
- Johnson Space Center Mission Control continues 24/7 flight operations. Flight controllers rotating through shifts; daily status briefings scheduled through mission conclusion.

### Science & Instruments

**Crew Health Experiments (ongoing)**
- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Organ-on-a-chip devices built from the **crew members' own bone marrow cells**, measuring effects of deep space radiation and microgravity on human tissue in real time. Bone marrow was chosen because it is both radiation-sensitive and central to immune function. Payload developed by Space Tango; battery-powered with automated environmental control. Crew checked out the AVATAR payload in flight on Flight Day 1.
  - Sources: https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/ | https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00964-4
- **ARCHeR (Artemis Research for Crew Health and Readiness):** Wristband actigraphy device worn by all crew members before, during, and after the mission. Monitors movement, sleep patterns, stress, cognitive performance, and team dynamics for real-time health monitoring by crew and flight controllers.

**CubeSat Science (post-deployment)**
- All four CubeSats confirmed deployed and operational after SLS stage adapter separation. No new operational data publicly released as of 07:00 UTC April 3.
- **K-RadCube (South Korea — KASA/KASI):** Measuring Van Allen Belt radiation levels.
- **Space Weather CubeSat-1 (Saudi Arabia — SSA):** Measuring radiation, solar X-rays, energetic particles, and magnetic fields.
- **TACHELES (Germany — DLR):** Demonstrating in-space technologies for future lunar logistics.
- **ATENEA (Argentina — CONAE):** Radiation shielding and long-range communications research.
  - Source: https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/04/02/satellite-cubesat/5711775174421/

**Upcoming Science Activities**
- Lunar far side photography: During the ~6-hour April 6 flyby window, the crew will photograph the far side under oblique illumination using the Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP) software (NTRS 20260001932) to guide observation of specific surface features — crater rims, ridges, slopes — whose relief is enhanced by shadow geometry. First humans to observe the far side with their own eyes in over 50 years.
  - Sources: https://science.nasa.gov/artemis-ii-science/ | https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260001932

## Flight Day 3 Addendum — New Findings (Search: 2026-04-03 ~07:00 UTC)

### Additional Anomalies

**PCD (Portable Computing Device) Failure — Open Issue**
Mission Specialist Christina Koch's Portable Computing Device (a Microsoft Surface Pro) went dark before the TLI burn and has remained unresponsive as of Flight Day 3. The PCD is the crew's interface for internet access and mission timeline tracking. Mission Control has provided troubleshooting steps; none have restored the device. No impact on mission-critical Orion systems has been reported.
- Sources: https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/01/science/live-news/artemis-2-nasa-launch | https://www.aol.com/articles/astronauts-artemis-ii-ran-problem-034040181.html

### Flight Day 3 Scheduled Activities (Confirmed via Daily Agenda)
- **OTC-1 (Outbound Trajectory Correction Burn 1):** First of three small trajectory refinement burns to keep Orion on the precise free-return lunar trajectory. Jeremy Hansen leads preparations; burn scheduled shortly after the crew's midday meal.
- **CPR in microgravity demonstration:** Glover, Koch, and Hansen will demonstrate CPR procedures in weightlessness.
- **Medical kit checkout:** Wiseman and Glover will inspect the onboard medical kit (thermometer, blood pressure monitor, stethoscope, otoscope).
- **DSN emergency comms test:** Koch is assigned to test Orion's emergency communications system on the Deep Space Network in the second half of the day.
- Source: https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/nasas-artemis-ii-moon-mission-daily-agenda/

### CubeSat Enhanced Status
- **K-RadCube (South Korea):** Confirmed operational; actively downlinking science data to Korea Astronomy and Space Science Institute (KASI) ground stations. Executing two-week mission of repeated Van Allen belt passes. Carries Samsung Electronics K-RAD-SS multi-chip semiconductor module and SK hynix K-RAD-SK memory chip for radiation tolerance evaluation.
  - Source: https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/04/02/satellite-cubesat/5711775174421/
- **TACHELES (Germany):** Being raised progressively to altitudes above 38,000 km (~24,000 mi), passing through both Van Allen belts. Carries HiveR rover microcontrollers distributed across three boards for single-event-effect detection, plus a shielded board and radiation sensors.
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacheles_(satellite)
- **ATENEA (Argentina):** Target operating altitude ~70,000 km from Earth — a record for Argentine space operations. Communicating with CONAE ground stations in Tierra del Fuego and Córdoba.
  - Source: https://en.mercopress.com/2026/03/31/argentine-microsatellite-to-fly-on-nasa-s-artemis-2-moon-mission

### King Charles III Letter to Jeremy Hansen
King Charles III sent a personal letter to Jeremy Hansen before launch, invoking the Astra Carta (an environmental charter for the space sector) and urging Hansen to carry the message of environmental stewardship from beyond Earth orbit. Key quotes from the letter:
- "It is with immense pride and a profound sense of shared purpose that I write to you as you prepare to embark upon the Artemis II mission – the first crewed voyage beyond low Earth orbit since the dawn of the Space Age."
- "In this historic moment, you stand as a bridge between nations and generations, and I commend you for the courage, discipline and vision that have brought you to this threshold."
- Charles urged Hansen to "reflect upon the fragile beauty of Earth as it rises above the lunar horizon" and to help ensure humanity cares for "the Universe, and all life contained within it, with reverence and a true sense of what is Sacred."
- Hansen responded: "I was deeply touched to receive this message, which honours the collective vision and dedication behind this historic moment."
