# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-07 22:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is transitioning into Flight Day 8, with TCB-1 (Return Trajectory Correction Burn 1) having been scheduled for ~01:03 UTC April 8 (9:03 PM ET April 7) — no post-burn anomalies have been reported and the splashdown target of April 10 remains firmly on track. The crew completed a cross-mission audio call with ISS Expedition 74 on April 7, and proposed naming two newly-observed lunar craters during the flyby. Flight Day 8 brings the second manual piloting demonstration, a first-ever radiation storm shelter drill, and Jeremy Hansen's third live CSA Q&A.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Active crewed mission — transitioning to Flight Day 8 of 10; trans-Earth coast; TCB-1 executed or imminent as of report time
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC, Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown Target:** April 10, 2026 at ~00:07 UTC (~8:07 p.m. EDT / 5:07 p.m. PDT April 9), Pacific Ocean ~50–80 miles off San Diego; recovery vessel **USS John P. Murtha (LPD 26)**

  - Crew revealed crater-naming proposal: "Integrity" (after their Orion capsule) and a second crater honoring Commander Wiseman's late wife, Carroll
  - Ship-to-ship ISS audio call confirmed for April 7 at 2:40 PM ET with Expedition 74 crew

### Technical & Engineering

- **TCB-1 — scheduled ~01:03 UTC April 8 (9:03 PM ET April 7):** The first return trajectory correction burn was described in the Flight Day 7 NASA blog as: "Orion's thrusters are planned to ignite at 9:03 p.m. for the first of three trajectory correction burns." No post-burn anomalies have been reported and the mission remains GO for splashdown April 10, consistent with nominal execution. Two outbound correction burns (TEC-2 and TEC-3) were skipped on the way to the Moon due to trajectory precision, but all three return burns (TCB-1, -2, -3) are currently planned.
- **TCB-2 and TCB-3 — scheduled April 9:** Two additional return trajectory correction burns planned to progressively fine-tune the re-entry corridor before entry interface on April 10.
- **Entry interface (April 10):** Orion targeting reentry at ~25,000 mph (~40,000 km/h) — highest-velocity crewed reentry ever attempted. All systems nominal.
- **Wastewater/toilet system:** Partial workaround remains in effect (contingency collapsible urinal devices / CCUs). Non-mission-impacting.
- **ESA European Service Module (ESM-2):** Operating nominally through trans-Earth coast. ESA engineers supporting around the clock from ESTEC (Netherlands) and EAC (Germany).

### Science & Instruments

- **Crater naming proposal (Flight Day 7):** During the lunar flyby debrief, the crew proposed naming two newly-observed far-side craters — one "Integrity" (after their Orion spacecraft) and a second honoring Commander Wiseman's late wife, Carroll. Formal IAU naming would require post-mission submission.
- **AVATAR (organ-on-a-chip):** Nominal; bone-marrow organ-chips from each crew member's own cells continue collecting deep-space radiation exposure data. Saliva sample collection by crew is ongoing as scheduled per the experiment protocol.
- **ARCHER (actigraphy):** Crew wristband health monitors are continuing to collect sleep, circadian rhythm, and activity data throughout the return phase.
- **HERA/CAD dosimetry:** Six Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessors and Crew Active Dosimeters continue recording accumulated dose. Post-peak-exposure phase; crew now in lower-radiation environment within Earth's magnetosphere approaches.
- **Radiation Storm Shelter Drill (Flight Day 8, ~8:15 PM ET):** Crew to demonstrate construction of a temporary radiation shelter inside Orion using on-board supplies — first-ever execution of this emergency protocol by a crew in deep space. Data will inform future Artemis and lunar exploration mission procedures.
- **Space Weather monitoring:** Solar radiation forecast AI model (University of Michigan) providing real-time risk assessment; no solar energetic particle events currently flagged.
  - [To Protect Artemis II Astronauts, NASA Experts Keep Eyes on Sun — NASA Science](https://science.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/to-protect-artemis-ii-astronauts-nasa-experts-keep-eyes-on-sun/)
