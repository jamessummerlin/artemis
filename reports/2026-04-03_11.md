# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03 11:00 UTC

## Summary
Artemis II is approximately 2.5 days into flight, currently on its outbound free-return trajectory to the Moon following a successful Translunar Injection burn on April 2. The crew conducted a media interview from space and continues nominal operations; lunar closest approach (~4,100 miles from the surface) and a new human distance-from-Earth record (~252,799 miles) are targeted for ~April 6. New NTRS technical reports and an MIT Lincoln Laboratory press release highlight the mission's optical laser communications experiment and CubeSat secondary payloads.

## Mission Status
- **Current Phase:** In-flight — translunar transit, Day 3 post-launch
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC (6:35 p.m. EDT), Launch Complex 39B
- **Lunar Closest Approach:** ~April 6, 2026 (~1:45 p.m. EDT); ~4,100 miles (6,600 km) from lunar surface
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10, 2026, Pacific Ocean off San Diego, CA
- **Notable Changes:** Gateway space station program canceled in March 2026; Artemis III revised to LEO technology demonstrations in mid-2027 before a lunar landing attempt; Artemis IV (crewed lunar landing) now targets 2028.

### Technical & Engineering

**SLS Rocket**
- SLS Block 1 generated 8.8 million lbs thrust at liftoff; solid rocket boosters provided >75% of initial thrust.
- Core stage MECO, booster separation, ICPS separation, and ICPS TLI firing all completed nominally.

**Orion Spacecraft ("Integrity")**
- All four solar array wings deployed; full wingspan ~63 feet.
- Proximity operations demonstration completed April 1 (Pilot Victor Glover manually maneuvered the vehicle relative to the ICPS target).
- Perigee raise burn completed April 1 with a 43-second service module engine firing.
- TLI burn completed April 2 (5 min 50 sec), placing Orion on trajectory to the Moon.
- Minor toilet fault light resolved April 2 in coordination with Mission Control.

**Heat Shield**
- Artemis I heat shield erosion (100+ AVCOAT block cracking sites) led NASA to eliminate the skip-reentry profile.
- Artemis II uses a **steeper, direct reentry at ~25,000 mph** — the fastest crewed reentry ever attempted — to minimize time in peak thermal environment.
- FRR on March 12, 2026 produced a unanimous "go"; post-flight inspection will be critical data for Artemis IV planning.
  - Sources: https://phys.org/news/2026-03-shield-safety-stakes-nasa-artemis.html | https://theconversation.com/heat-shield-safety-concerns-raise-stakes-for-nasas-artemis-ii-moon-mission-275853

**Trajectory**
- Free-return trajectory (no lunar orbit insertion); similar profile to Apollo 13.
- Maximum distance from Earth: **~252,799 miles (406,841 km)**, exceeding Apollo 13's ~248,655-mile record.
- Closest lunar approach: ~4,100 miles (6,600 km) from the surface, ~April 6.
- Splashdown in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego, CA; U.S. Navy San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock assigned to recovery.
  - Sources: https://www.nasa.gov/mission/artemis-ii/ | https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/local/artemis-ii-mission-splash-down-off-san-diegos-coast/4001670/

**Optical Communications**
- O2O (Orion Artemis II Optical Communications) MIT Lincoln Laboratory laser terminal onboard Orion is operational.
  - Downlinks up to **260 Mbps** including 4K HD video from lunar distances.
  - Ground stations: NASA White Sands Complex (NM) and Table Mountain Facility (CA).
  - Sources: https://news.mit.edu/2026/lincoln-laboratory-laser-communications-terminal-launches-artemis-ii-0402 | https://www.nasa.gov/directorates/somd/space-communications-navigation-program/o2o/ | NTRS: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250010065

### Science & Instruments

**Crew Health & Biology**
- **AVATAR:** Organ-on-a-chip devices using crew members' own bone marrow cells; measures combined effects of radiation and microgravity. Blood and saliva samples will track immune system changes throughout flight.
  - Source: https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/
- **ARCHeR:** Wearable watch-like device monitoring crew activity, well-being, and sleep patterns.

**Radiation Monitoring**
- **HERA (Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessors):** Six active radiation sensors inside the Orion crew module; builds on Artemis I dataset.
- **Crew Active Dosimeters:** Each astronaut wears a pocket personal dosimeter.
- Solar superflare risk: Mission launches during elevated solar activity (mid-2025 to mid-2026 solar maximum period); NASA solar weather monitoring teams active around the clock.
  - Source: https://www.livescience.com/space/space-exploration/astronauts-can-face-nearly-lethal-doses-of-solar-radiation-so-why-launch-artemis-ii-during-the-suns-peak-of-activity-space-scientist-patricia-reiff-explains

**CubeSat Secondary Payloads (deployed ~5 hours post-launch)**
- **K-RadCube (Korea Aerospace Administration / KASI):** Human-tissue-equivalent dosimeter in high elliptical Earth orbit, measuring radiation across Van Allen Belts. Confirmed operational.
  - Source: https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/04/02/satellite-cubesat/5711775174421/
- **Space Weather CubeSat-1 (Saudi Space Agency):** Measuring radiation, solar X-rays, solar energetic particles, and magnetic fields.
- **TACHELES (DLR, Germany):** In-space technology demonstration for future lunar logistics vehicles.
  - Source: https://www.dlr.de/en/latest/news/2026/artemis-ii-to-launch-for-the-moon-with-german-and-european-tech-on-board
- **ATENEA (CONAE, Argentina):** Radiation shielding research, orbital design optimization, and long-range communications.
  - Source: https://digitalcommons.usu.edu/smallsat/2025/E-S2-2025/1/

**Lunar Observations**
- Crew follows a **Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP)** developed by the Crew Lunar Observations (CLO) team for systematic surface imaging at closest approach, focusing on craters, lava flows, and far-side terrain.
  - NTRS Paper: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260001932

## Upcoming Milestones
- **~April 6, 2026 (~1:45 p.m. EDT):** Orion reaches ~252,799 miles from Earth (new human spaceflight distance record); lunar closest approach (~4,100 miles); crew photographs lunar far side.
- **~April 10, 2026:** Planned splashdown, Pacific Ocean off San Diego; U.S. Navy recovery.
- **Post-mission:** Heat shield post-flight inspection — critical data point for Artemis IV design.
- **Mid-2027:** Artemis III — crewed mission to low Earth orbit for rendezvous and docking tests with commercial HLS landers (SpaceX and Blue Origin). No lunar landing attempt.
- **~2028:** Artemis IV — first crewed lunar landing since Apollo 17.
- **Canceled:** Lunar Gateway space station (canceled by NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman, March 2026); hardware redirected toward a concept for a permanent lunar surface base.
