# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-07 15:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 7 of 10, continuing trans-Earth coast with all systems nominal. Since the 11:00 UTC report, the crew completed a live call to ISS astronauts and a science debrief with JSC lunar teams covering the 35 geological observations made during the April 6 flyby. Orion is approximately 2.5 hours from exiting the lunar sphere of influence (~17:25 UTC) and remains on course for April 10 splashdown off San Diego.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Active crewed mission — Flight Day 7 of 10; trans-Earth coast; all flyby objectives complete
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC, Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown Target:** April 10, 2026 at ~00:07 UTC (~8:07 p.m. EDT / 5:07 p.m. PDT April 9), Pacific Ocean ~50–80 miles off San Diego; recovery vessel **USS John P. Murtha (LPD 26)**
- **Notable Changes Since 11:00 UTC Report:**
  - ISS crew call completed — first-ever simultaneous contact between crewed missions at different orbital altitudes
  - Science debrief with JSC lunar teams completed; ~35 CLO geological observations transmitted and reviewed
  - Lunar sphere of influence exit milestone (~17:25 UTC today, at ~41,072 miles from Moon) approaching but not yet reached at report time
  - Additional NTRS technical reports and new media coverage indexed since morning
  - No new anomalies; all systems nominal

### Technical & Engineering

- **Lunar sphere of influence exit (imminent — ~17:25 UTC today):** At approximately 17:25 UTC (1:25 p.m. EDT), Orion exits the lunar sphere of influence at ~41,072 miles from the Moon and transitions to Earth-dominated gravitational pull. All trans-Earth coast telemetry remains nominal ahead of this milestone.
- **O2O Optical Communications System — new detail (MIT Lincoln Lab / Scientific American):** The MAScOT (Moon to Space Optical Communications Terminal) terminal aboard Orion — described as roughly the size of a house cat — is achieving up to **260 Mbps downlink and 20 Mbps uplink**, beaming 4K video from cislunar space. Ground receiving stations are at NASA's White Sands Complex (New Mexico) and Table Mountain Facility (California). The system is a Detailed Test Objective (non-mission-critical).
  - [NASA's Artemis II laser communications system beaming 4K video — Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/nasas-artemis-ii-laser-communications-system-is-beaming-4k-video-from-the/)
  - [Lincoln Laboratory laser comms terminal on Artemis II — MIT News](https://news.mit.edu/2026/lincoln-laboratory-laser-communications-terminal-launches-artemis-ii-0402)
- **Radiation shelter demo scheduled for Flight Day 8 (April 8):** Crew will construct a radiation storm shelter using onboard supplies and equipment, simulating rapid protective response to a solar energetic particle event — first time this contingency procedure has been tested with crew in deep space.
- **K-RadCube (South Korea/KASA) confirmed failed:** The South Korean CubeSat deployed from the SLS upper stage has been confirmed lost following deployment. The Seoul Economic Daily reported that Korean partners are treating the partial mission data as a learning opportunity for future space missions.
  - [K-RadCube Fails to Survive — Seoul Economic Daily](https://en.sedaily.com/finance/2026/04/04/k-radcube-fails-to-survive-but-korean-partners-gain-space)
- **Wastewater/toilet system:** Partial fix remains in effect; crew using contingency collapsible urinal devices (CCUs). Non-mission-impacting.
- **Entry interface (April 10):** Orion targeting reentry at ~25,000 mph (~40,000 km/h) — highest-velocity crewed reentry ever attempted. Post-splashdown heat shield inspection planned.

### Science & Instruments

- **Crew Lunar Observations (CLO) debrief — completed Flight Day 7:** Crew briefed JSC lunar science teams on all 35 geological observations made during the April 6 flyby, including color variation mapping (for mineralogy inference), far-side feature identification, Earthrise photography, and 57 minutes of solar corona imagery collected during totality (00:35–01:32 UTC April 7). This is the first human visual survey of far-side lunar features in history.
- **Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP) detail:** The CLO team's Lunar Targeting Plan pre-designated observation targets; crew worked in paired observation shifts using handheld Nikon D5 cameras and provided real-time audio descriptions to Mission Control. The 10 formal science objectives included color variation mapping across multiple terrane types to infer mineralogy without instruments.
- **Solar eclipse science — detail:** During the 57-minute totality the crew observed stars, Venus, Mars, Saturn, and Earth's glow — an unprecedented observational geometry. Solar physics teams at JSC received real-time crew audio.
- **AVATAR (organ-on-a-chip):** Experiment continues nominal; radiation exposure measurements for the deep-space transit are near-complete as Orion re-enters Earth's magnetosphere zone over the coming days.
- **HERA/CAD radiation monitoring:** Six Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessors (HERAs) and Crew Active Dosimeters (CADs) continue recording. Germany's DLR M-42 EXT sensor also operating. Peak deep-space exposure phase ended post-flyby; data analysis to follow splashdown.
- **Space Weather CubeSat-1 (Saudi Arabia):** Measuring radiation, solar X-rays, solar energetic particles, and magnetic fields in highly elliptical Earth orbit. Nominal.
- **ATENEA (Argentina/CONAE) and TACHELES (Germany/DLR) CubeSats:** Both continue operating nominally.
