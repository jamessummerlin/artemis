# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-06 11:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 6 of 10, approximately 7.75 hours before the lunar flyby observation window opens at 18:45 UTC. Orion and all four crew members remain healthy on a nominal free-return trajectory inside the Moon's gravitational sphere of influence. No new NASA blog posts have been published since the 10:00 UTC cycle; the major flyby milestones — closest approach (~4,070 miles above the surface), Apollo 13 distance record break, and the planned 40-minute communications blackout — are on track and imminent.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Active crewed mission — Flight Day 6 of 10; inside lunar sphere of influence; pre-flyby coast (flyby observation window opens 18:45 UTC)
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC from Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown Target:** April 10, 2026 ~00:07 UTC (5:07 p.m. PDT April 9) off the coast of San Diego; recovery by USS San Diego
- **Notable Program-Level Changes:**
  - Lunar Gateway program canceled (March 2026); resources redirected toward an Artemis Base Camp on the lunar surface
  - Artemis III revised: no lunar landing; will perform LEO rendezvous/docking tests in 2027 with SpaceX Starship HLS and/or Blue Origin Blue Moon, including AxEMU spacesuit testing
  - Lunar landing deferred to Artemis IV (~early 2028)
  - SLS Exploration Upper Stage (EUS) canceled (February 2026); ULA Centaur V selected as alternative upper stage for Artemis IV+

### Technical & Engineering

- **Trajectory:** Orion is on a multi-trans-lunar injection (MTLI) free-return trajectory. The final outbound trajectory correction burn was completed at 03:03 UTC April 6 (11:03 p.m. EDT April 5). No further propulsive maneuvers are required until post-flyby return corrections.
  - [Artemis II Flight Day 5: Correction Burn Complete](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/05/artemis-ii-flight-day-5-correction-burn-complete/)
- **Flyby parameters:** Closest approach of ~4,070 miles (6,550 km) above the lunar surface expected at approximately 23:02 UTC; maximum distance from Earth (~252,757 miles / 406,870 km) at ~23:07 UTC — surpassing the Apollo 13 crew record of 248,655 miles set in 1970.
- **Communications blackout:** A planned ~40-minute DSN blackout begins at ~22:44 UTC as Orion passes behind the Moon. Communications expected to resume at ~23:25 UTC at "Earthrise."
- **Spacecraft systems:** All systems are nominal. No anomalies have been reported since the toilet system (Universal Waste Management System) was successfully troubleshot on Flight Day 2.
  - [Crew and Ground Teams Successfully Troubleshoot Orion's Toilet](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/02/artemis-ii-flight-update-crew-and-ground-teams-successfully-troubleshoot-orions-toilet/)
- **Heat shield:** Orion's modified reentry angle remains the planned mitigation for the Artemis I heat shield char loss anomaly; no additional hardware changes.
- **ESA European Service Module (ESM):** Propulsion (33 engines), power (four solar array wings), thermal control, and life support systems continue performing nominally. ESA engineers at ESTEC and NASA JSC maintain 24/7 monitoring.

### Science & Instruments

- **Crew Lunar Observations (CLO):** During the flyby window (18:45–01:40 UTC), the crew will document ~35 geological targets briefed by Mission Control science teams, including:
  - Orientale Basin (~600 miles wide, straddling near and far sides)
  - Ancient lava flows (mare basalts) on the near side
  - Tectonic features (ridges and scarps from crustal cooling)
  - Color and brightness variations indicating mineralogical composition
  - The far side of the Moon — regions never seen directly by humans
  - Crew will work in pairs, providing real-time verbal descriptions to scientists in Mission Control — the first systematic crewed lunar geological survey in more than 50 years.
  - [NASA Artemis II Lunar Science Operations](https://science.nasa.gov/solar-system/nasas-artemis-ii-lunar-science-operations-to-inform-future-missions/)
- **Solar corona and meteoroid observations:** At ~00:35 UTC April 7, the Sun will pass behind the Moon from Orion's perspective. The crew will image the solar corona for approximately one hour and observe for meteoroid impact flashes on the lunar surface.
- **AVATAR investigation (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Organ-on-a-chip devices containing bone marrow cells derived from crew blood samples are operating continuously, studying deep-space radiation and microgravity effects on the human immune system.
  - [Avatars for Astronaut Health to Fly on NASA's Artemis II](https://science.nasa.gov/directorates/smd/avatars-for-astronaut-health-nasa-artemis-ii/)
- **ARCHeR investigation:** Continuous real-time monitoring of crew performance, teamwork, sleep, and behavioral health via wrist monitors and saliva samples.
- **CubeSat secondary payloads:** Deployed five hours after launch, CubeSats from Germany (TACHELES), South Korea, Saudi Arabia, and Argentina continue operating in cislunar space.
  - [NTRS: Hitching a Ride on History: NASA's SLS Artemis II CubeSats](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250005942)
