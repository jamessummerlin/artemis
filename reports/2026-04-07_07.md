# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-07 07:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 7 of 10, cruising back toward Earth after a successful lunar flyby completed on Flight Day 6. Orion is in trans-Earth coast with all systems nominal; the crew is healthy and conducting scheduled science debriefs and a live call with the International Space Station crew. The spacecraft is expected to exit the lunar sphere of influence at ~17:25 UTC today, approximately 41,072 miles from the Moon, as it targets an April 10 splashdown off San Diego.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Active crewed mission — Flight Day 7 of 10; trans-Earth cruise; lunar flyby and all flyby science objectives complete
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC, Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown Target:** April 10, 2026 at ~00:07 UTC (~8:07 p.m. EDT April 9), Pacific Ocean ~50 miles off coast of San Diego; recovery vessel USS San Diego
- **Notable Changes Since 05:00 UTC Report:**
  - Orion continuing nominal trans-Earth coast; no anomalies reported
  - Crew science debrief with NASA Johnson Space Center scientists underway
  - Live crew call to ISS scheduled for today (Flight Day 7)
  - Orion to exit lunar sphere of influence at ~17:25 UTC at ~41,072 miles from the Moon
  - Wastewater/toilet system: partial fix remains in effect; crew continues use of contingency collapsible urinals (CCUs)

### Technical & Engineering

- **Trans-Earth coast underway:** Orion is on the return leg of its free-return trajectory. No trajectory correction burns were required during lunar closest approach — the spacecraft's path was sufficiently precise.
- **Lunar sphere of influence exit:** At approximately **17:25 UTC** (1:25 p.m. EDT), Orion will exit the lunar sphere of influence at a distance of **41,072 miles from the Moon**, transitioning to Earth-dominated gravitational influence.
- **Spacecraft systems:** All systems remain nominal as of the latest downlinked telemetry. Entry interface for splashdown is targeted at ~25,000 mph (~40,000 km/h) — the fastest crewed reentry velocity ever attempted — with heat shield performance to be closely evaluated post-recovery.
- **Wastewater/toilet system status:** Mission controllers earlier identified a suspected ice blockage in the urine vent line. Heaters and spacecraft rotation to face the vent toward the Sun achieved a partial fix — approximately half the urine tank was emptied. The crew continues using individual contingency collapsible urinals (CCUs) as the primary waste management system; the issue is not mission-impacting.
  - [NASA Blog: Crew and Ground Teams Successfully Troubleshoot Orion's Toilet](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/02/artemis-ii-flight-update-crew-and-ground-teams-successfully-troubleshoot-orions-toilet/)
  - [CNN: Artemis II crew grappled with a toilet problem](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/04/science/artemis-2-toilet-malfunction)
- **Distance record confirmed:** Maximum distance from Earth reached at ~23:07 UTC April 6 — **252,757 miles (406,772 km)**, surpassing the Apollo 13 record of 248,655 miles by ~4,102 miles.
- **SLS performance:** Both solid rocket boosters and all four RS-25 engines performed nominally at launch, generating 8.8 million pounds of thrust at liftoff. Translunar injection burn fired successfully ~6 minutes on April 2.
  - [EDI Weekly: Artemis II Engineering Update — SLS Rollout, Trajectory Refinements](https://www.ediweekly.com/artemis-ii-engineering-update-sls-rollout-trajectory-refinements-and-hardware-readiness-for-the-april-2026-launch-window/)

### Science & Instruments

- **Crew Lunar Observations (CLO) — complete:** All 35 pre-identified geological targets were observed and described by the crew during the Flight Day 6 flyby window. Imagery and crew audio descriptions have been downlinked to the science team at Johnson Space Center. Post-mission analysis of photography from Orion's 32 cameras is planned after splashdown.
  - [CNN: Why Artemis II's lunar flyby will offer unprecedented views](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/06/science/artemis-2-lunar-flyby-geology)
- **Solar corona observations:** During a 57-minute total solar eclipse (00:35–01:32 UTC April 7) caused by Orion's position beyond the Moon, the crew captured solar corona imagery and verbally described corona features to solar physics teams on the ground. The zodiacal light was also searched for during totality. These observations are the longest crewed solar corona observations ever conducted beyond Earth orbit.
  - [Space.com: Artemis 2 LIVE — Solar eclipse ends for astronauts at the Moon](https://www.space.com/news/live/artemis-2-nasa-moon-mission-updates-april-6-2026)
- **AVATAR investigation (organ-on-a-chip):** The AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response) experiment continues operating nominally. Organ-on-a-chip devices seeded with cells from each astronaut's blood are measuring deep-space radiation and microgravity effects on bone marrow analogs. The peak radiation exposure phase (beyond Earth's magnetosphere) is now ending as Orion returns toward Earth.
  - [NASA Science: AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response)](https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/)
- **Radiation monitoring:** Six Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessors (HERAs) and individual Crew Active Dosimeters (CADs) are measuring radiation throughout the mission. Germany's DLR contributed the M-42 EXT sensor — six times more sensitive than the Artemis I version — monitoring total dose and particle types including heavy ions.
- **Optical communications (O2O):** Laser communications experiment continues operating nominally in cislunar space.
- **Science debrief:** Crew is debriefing with JSC science teams on Flight Day 7 observations, including CLO imagery, corona observations, and Earthrise photography.
  - [NPR: Artemis II astronauts heading home after historic moon flyby](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/06/nx-s1-5773187/nasa-artemis-ii-moon-lunar-flyby)

### International Partners

- **ESA — European Service Module (ESM-2):** All 33 engines and four solar array wings continue operating nominally. The ESM is providing power, propulsion, and life support for the Orion crew module throughout the trans-Earth cruise. ESA representatives from the Space Medicine Team are observing mission operations alongside NASA, CSA, and JAXA partners.
  - [ESA: Artemis II mission begins](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Artemis_II_mission_begins)
  - [ESA: Europe powers Artemis II mission to the Moon](https://www.esa.int/Newsroom/Press_Releases/Europe_powers_Artemis_II_mission_to_the_Moon)
  - The ESM was built with contributions from 10 European countries, 20 main contractors, and 100+ European suppliers.
- **CSA — Jeremy Hansen:** Hansen is the first non-U.S. citizen and first Canadian to reach the lunar vicinity. CBC News carried extensive coverage of the flyby and distance record.
  - [CBC: Artemis II crew breaks distance record, loops around the Moon and heads home](https://www.cbc.ca/news/science/artemis-lunar-flyby-apollo-13-record-9.7153912)
  - CSA Q&A session #3 with Canadians scheduled for Flight Day 8.
- **JAXA:** JAXA representatives are observing mission operations as part of the international team at JSC.
- **International CubeSats:** Four CubeSats deployed to highly elliptical Earth orbit during the mission — from Germany (TACHELES), South Korea (K-Rad Cube), Saudi Arabia, and Argentina (ATENEA) — continue operating nominally.
- **Artemis III / Program Update:** NASA announced in March 2026 that the Lunar Gateway program has been canceled in its current form, with resources redirected to permanent lunar surface infrastructure. Artemis III (now targeting 2027) has been revised to conduct LEO rendezvous and docking tests with SpaceX Starship HLS and/or Blue Origin Blue Moon, plus AxEMU spacesuit testing, rather than a lunar landing. The first crewed lunar landing is now targeted for Artemis IV (~early 2028).
  - [Artemis III - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_III)
