# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-06 09:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 6, with Orion cruising through the lunar sphere of influence ahead of today's historic lunar flyby window (2:45–9:40 p.m. EDT / 18:45–01:40 UTC). The crew is healthy and actively preparing for closest approach at ~4,070 miles above the lunar surface (~7:02 p.m. EDT / 23:02 UTC), at which point they will surpass the Apollo 13 distance record by 4,102 miles. Separately, newly surfaced pre-launch program details confirm that NASA canceled the Exploration Upper Stage (EUS) in February 2026 and selected ULA's Centaur V as an alternative upper stage for Artemis IV and beyond.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Active crewed mission — Flight Day 6 of 10; inside lunar sphere of influence; pre-flyby approach phase
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 6:35 p.m. EDT (22:35:12 UTC) from Launch Complex 39B, KSC
- **Splashdown Target:** April 10, 2026 ~5:07 p.m. PDT off San Diego coast; recovery by USS San Diego
- **Notable Changes:**
  - Lunar Gateway program canceled (March 2026)
  - Artemis III revised: no lunar landing; will perform LEO rendezvous/docking tests in 2027 with SpaceX Starship HLS and/or Blue Origin Blue Moon; AxEMU spacesuit testing included
  - Lunar landing deferred to Artemis IV (~early 2028)
  - SLS Exploration Upper Stage (EUS) canceled (February 2026); ULA Centaur V selected as alternative upper stage for Artemis IV+

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- **Lunar sphere of influence:** Orion entered the Moon's gravitational sphere of influence at 12:41 a.m. EDT (04:41 UTC) on April 6. As of 09:00 UTC the spacecraft is approximately 220,000 miles from Earth, closing on the Moon ahead of flyby.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/05/artemis-ii-flight-day-5-crew-demos-suits-readies-for-lunar-flyby/
- **Flyby window:** The lunar observation window opens at 2:45 p.m. EDT (18:45 UTC) and closes at 9:40 p.m. EDT (01:40 UTC April 7). Closest approach of 4,070 miles above the surface occurs at approximately 7:02 p.m. EDT (23:02 UTC).
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasa-sets-coverage-for-artemis-ii-moon-mission/
- **Distance record:** The crew is expected to break the Apollo 13 record of 248,655 miles at ~1:56 p.m. EDT (17:56 UTC), reaching a maximum of 252,757 miles from Earth at ~7:07 p.m. EDT (23:07 UTC).
  - Source: https://www.space.com/news/live/artemis-2-nasa-moon-mission-updates-april-5-2026
- **Live coverage:** NASA TV and the NASA app are carrying live coverage throughout the flyby.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasa-sets-coverage-for-artemis-ii-moon-mission/
- **Crew preparation:** Per NPR reporting, the crew spent the morning of April 6 reviewing observation targets, positioning cameras, and conducting pre-flyby briefings with Mission Control.
  - Source: https://www.npr.org/2026/04/06/nx-s1-5773187/nasa-artemis-ii-moon-lunar-flyby

### Technical & Engineering

- **Trajectory and free-return profile:** Orion is on a multi-trans-lunar injection (MTLI) free-return trajectory — a figure-eight path around the Moon. No propulsive burn is required for the return journey; lunar gravity will slingshot the spacecraft back toward Earth. Final outbound trajectory correction burn was completed at 11:03 p.m. EDT April 5.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/05/artemis-ii-flight-day-5-correction-burn-complete/
- **Communications blackout:** A planned ~40-minute comm blackout will occur as Orion passes behind the Moon, blocking Deep Space Network contact; no crew action is required.
- **Orion spacecraft mass and capability:** Post-TLI separation mass of ~26,375 kg (58,147 lb); delta-v capability up to 1,050 m/s. All four solar array wings deployed; spacecraft systems nominal.
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)
- **Pre-launch anomaly resolved:** A helium flow problem on the SLS upper stage discovered February 21 forced a return to the VAB, delaying launch from March to April. Flight termination system batteries on all stages were also replaced during the additional processing period. Both issues were resolved prior to the April 1 launch.
  - Source: https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/03/19/live-coverage-nasa-to-roll-its-sls-rocket-back-to-the-launch-pad-ahead-of-planned-april-flight-of-artemis-2/
- **SLS EUS cancellation / Centaur V selection:** In February 2026, NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman announced cancellation of the Exploration Upper Stage (EUS). NASA will standardize on the SLS Block 1 configuration and use ULA's Centaur V as the upper stage for Artemis IV and subsequent missions.
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Launch_System
- **Orion heat shield:** Modified reentry angle remains the planned mitigation for the Artemis I char loss anomaly; no additional hardware changes were made.

### Science & Instruments

- **Crew Lunar Observations (CLO):** The crew has 35 specific geological targets for the flyby window, including impact craters, ancient lava flows, surface ridges, and color/brightness variations to characterize surface composition. Observations are conducted in pairs with real-time verbal descriptions to Mission Control — the first systematic crewed lunar geological survey in over 50 years.
  - Source: https://science.nasa.gov/solar-system/nasas-artemis-ii-lunar-science-operations-to-inform-future-missions/
- **Far-side views:** Orientale basin — the ~600-mile-wide impact crater straddling the near and far sides — will be imaged in its entirety for the first time with human eyes.
  - Source: https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/what-will-nasas-artemis-ii-astronauts-see-on-the-moon/
- **Solar corona and meteoroid observations:** At ~8:35 p.m. EDT (00:35 UTC April 7), the Sun will pass behind the Moon from Orion's perspective. The crew will image the solar corona for approximately one hour and monitor for meteoroid impact flashes on the lunar surface.
  - Source: https://www.wzzm13.com/article/news/nation-world/artemis-ii-moon-mission-crew-distance-record-apollo-orion-capsule-loop-total-solar-eclipse/507-b3457f3c-b9c8-44ca-9346-2a6305f9e08b
- **Imaging assets:** Orion carries 32 cameras and imaging devices (interior and exterior), including handheld cameras with 80–400mm and 14–24mm lenses configured specifically for the flyby.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/artemis-ii-crew-to-advance-human-spaceflight-research/
- **ARCHeR (Artemis Research for Crew Health and Readiness):** Monitors crew performance, teamwork, mobility, sleep, and behavioral health in real time via wrist monitors and saliva samples.
- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Organ-on-a-chip devices studying deep-space radiation and microgravity effects on human tissue, operating continuously.
- **O2O laser comm, Space Weather CubeSat-1, ATENEA, TACHELES:** All secondary payloads nominal (see earlier daily reports for full details).
  - Source: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250006514

### International Partners

- **ESA (European Service Module):** ESM continues performing nominally across all subsystems — propulsion (33 engines), power (4 solar arrays), thermal control, and life support. ESA confirmed the ESM main engine executed the TLI burn. ESA engineers at ESTEC (Netherlands) and NASA JSC Mission Evaluation Room maintain 24/7 monitoring.
  - Source: https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/European_Service_Module_engines_powering_Artemis_II
- **CSA:** Jeremy Hansen continues active participation in lunar observations and crew activities. CSA is publishing daily mission logbook entries.
  - Source: https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/missions/artemis-ii/daily-logbook.asp
- **Artemis III program change impact:** The revised Artemis III architecture (LEO docking/suit test, no lunar landing) affects ESA, CSA, JAXA, and other international partners who had planned contributions tied to lunar surface operations. Lunar Gateway cancellation further changes the partnership framework; Canada's Canadarm3 contribution was tied to a Gateway mission slot for a future Canadian astronaut.
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_III
