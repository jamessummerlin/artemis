# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-02 12:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 2 with the crew in a high Earth orbit checkout loop following a flawless launch on April 1 at 22:35 UTC. The critical Trans-Lunar Injection (TLI) burn — a ~6-minute, 5-second firing of the ESM orbital maneuvering system engine — is scheduled for this evening (April 2, approximately 23:35–00:00 UTC / ~7:30–8:00 PM EDT), roughly 25 hours after liftoff. As of the time of this report, the TLI burn has not yet occurred; sources consistently indicate it is imminent and contingent on a final "go/no-go" from flight controllers after reviewing Day 1 system health data. Two technical anomalies have been noted: a ~10-minute partial communications dropout about 51 minutes into the flight (now resolved), and a controller issue with the onboard waste management system (toilet) flagged during post-launch briefings, expected to take a few hours to troubleshoot.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** In-flight — Flight Day 2; TLI burn pending (scheduled evening April 2 EDT)
- **Launched:** April 1, 2026 at 6:35 PM EDT (22:35 UTC) from LC-39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Current Orbit:** High Earth checkout orbit, apogee ~43,760 miles (70,403 km) — highest astronauts have flown since Apollo 17
- **TLI Burn Status:** NOT YET EXECUTED as of ~12:00 UTC April 2; scheduled for ~23:35 UTC April 2
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10–11, 2026, Pacific Ocean off San Diego

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- **Liftoff confirmed:** SLS Block 1 / Orion *Integrity* lifted off April 1 at 22:35 UTC generating ~8.8 million lbs thrust. All mission events through ICPS separation nominal. [NASA press release](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/liftoff-nasa-launches-astronauts-on-historic-artemis-moon-mission/)
- **Perigee raise maneuver complete:** ICPS RL10 engine fired to raise the lowest point of Orion's initial Earth orbit. [NASA blog — April 1](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/01/artemis-ii-flight-update-perigee-raise-maneuver-complete-nasa-to-hold-press-conference/)
- **Apogee raise burn complete:** Second ICPS burn (~18 minutes) raised apogee to ~43,760 miles. Crew now in high-Earth checkout orbit. [NASA blog — April 1](https://www.nasa.gov/blogs/missions/2026/04/01/artemis-ii-flight-update-apogee-raise-burn-complete-crew-looks-ahead-to-proximity-operations/)
- **Daily mission status briefings begin April 2** from NASA Johnson Space Center, except April 6 (lunar flyby). [NASA coverage page](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasa-sets-coverage-for-artemis-ii-moon-mission/)
- **TLI burn "go/no-go" decision:** Flight controllers are reviewing all Day 1 system health data this morning to confirm the spacecraft and crew are ready to commit to the lunar trajectory. The decision must be made before the burn window.

### Technical & Engineering

- **TLI Burn Profile (Scheduled — Not Yet Complete):**
  - Burn duration: ~6 minutes, 5 seconds
  - Engine: ESM orbital maneuvering system engine (~6,000 lbs thrust)
  - Delta-V: ~900 mph added velocity
  - Trajectory type: Free-return, figure-eight cislunar path (no Lunar Orbit Insertion required)
  - Profile: Multi-translunar injection (MTLI) — multiple departure burns used due to additional hydrogen boil-off from extended LEO parking compared to Artemis I
  - This is the **last major engine firing** of the Artemis II mission; all subsequent course corrections will be minor trajectory correction burns (TCBs)
  - Post-TLI: Orion begins a ~4-day coast to the Moon

- **Proximity Operations Demonstration (Completed — Flight Day 1):**
  - ~3.5 hours after launch, Pilot Victor Glover manually flew Orion around the ICPS in a ~70-minute demonstration
  - Orion maneuvered to ~300 ft, then ~30 ft from the ICPS using reaction control system thrusters
  - Docking camera data gathered will inform future autonomous rendezvous algorithms for Artemis III/IV
  - ICPS subsequently performed a disposal burn and deorbited over the Pacific Ocean

- **Deep Space Network handover:** Before TLI, Orion must complete a planned communications handover from NASA's Near Space Network to the Deep Space Network, tested at apogee.

- **Free-return trajectory specifics:**
  - Closest lunar approach: ~6,513 km from the far-side surface
  - Maximum distance from Earth: ~252,799 statute miles — expected to exceed the Apollo 13 record (~248,655 miles)
  - Reentry speed: ~25,000 mph; modified to a steeper descent angle (no skip reentry) to protect against heat shield spalling observed on Artemis I

- **NTRS technical report — Trajectory Correction Burns:** The Woffinden/Eckman/Robinson NTRS paper (20230000223) describing the optimization methodology for Artemis II TCBs is now directly applicable as mission controllers plan the minor post-TLI corrections scheduled for Days 3 and 4. [NTRS 20230000223](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)

- **SLS program status:** A December 2025 NTRS report (20250011679) confirms hardware for Artemis III, IV, and V is in production. Exploration Upper Stage test articles for the SLS Block 1B are undergoing testing at Marshall Space Flight Center. [NTRS 20250011679](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250011679)

### Technical Anomalies

- **Communications dropout (~51 minutes into flight):** About 51 minutes post-liftoff, during a planned satellite handover, Orion experienced a partial communications loss with Mission Control lasting approximately 10 minutes. Mission Control continued speaking to the crew "in the blind." Commander Reid Wiseman confirmed upon restoration that the crew could hear Mission Control throughout. NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman stated post-launch that this was investigated and is not considered a critical issue.

- **Waste management system (toilet) controller issue:** NASA Associate Administrator Amit Kshatriya disclosed at the post-launch press conference that a controller issue arose with the onboard toilet when it was first spun up. He estimated a few hours of troubleshooting. This is classified as a minor crew comfort issue; NASA noted this is a test mission and first time many Orion life support systems have been operated in space with crew.

- **Pre-launch anomalies resolved:** A Flight Termination System issue that briefly placed the mission in "No-Go" status was resolved before launch. An LAS battery temperature exceedance prior to launch was assessed as an instrumentation issue.

### Science & Instruments

- **Mission primary focus:** Systems validation — Orion life support, navigation, communications, and deep-space operations with crew. No science payloads land on the Moon.
- **Lunar far-side observations:** During the April 6 flyby, the crew will photograph and observe portions of the lunar far side — first humans to directly view some of these regions.
- **Optical communications test:** The Orion Optical Communications System (O2O) will transmit data at up to 260 Mbps, enabling near-real-time 4K video of the Moon.
- **Human research:** Crew performing radiation monitoring and physiological/human factors data collection throughout the mission.
- **Proximity ops navigation data:** Docking camera data from the ICPS maneuvers feeds future autonomous rendezvous algorithm development.

### Crew Updates

- **Commander Reid Wiseman:** Shortly after liftoff stated, *"We have a beautiful moonrise — we're headed right at it."* Overseeing all spacecraft operations and the go/no-go TLI decision.
- **Pilot Victor Glover:** Manually flew Orion during proximity operations on Day 1; exclaimed, *"I see it. Look at that, woohoo! I see the ICPS and the moon in the field of view."* First person of color to travel to the lunar vicinity.
- **Mission Specialist Christina Koch:** Spent the morning of April 2 preparing Orion's systems for the TLI burn. She will become the first woman to travel to the lunar vicinity.
- **Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA):** On his first spaceflight; first Canadian and first non-U.S. citizen ever to participate in a lunar mission. First live space-to-ground Q&A with Hansen is scheduled for April 4. [CSA event page](https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/events/2026/2026-04-04-artemis-ii-first-q-and-a-jeremy-hansen-live-from-space.asp)
- **First crew video call:** The first of several planned space-to-ground video communications is expected to take place on Flight Day 2, following TLI.

### International Partners

**ESA:**
- The European Service Module (ESM-2) — built by Airbus, integrating contributions from 20+ companies across 10 ESA member states — is performing nominally in-flight.
- ESA engineers are on console at ESTEC (Netherlands), ESOC (Germany), and JSC Houston monitoring the mission around the clock.
- An ESA blog post from March 25, 2026 (["From Europe to the Moon: ESM-2's Journey"](https://blogs.esa.int/orion/2026/03/25/from-europe-to-the-moon-esm-2s-journey/)) detailed the ESM's pre-launch integration milestones.
- ESM technical specs: 1 main engine + 8 auxiliary engines + 24 RCS thrusters; four 7-metre solar arrays generating 11.2 kW; 90 kg oxygen + 240 kg drinking water for life support (first time ESM life support is active with crew).
- Future ESMs: ESM-3 delivered for Artemis III testing; ESM-4 at KSC; ESM-5 and ESM-6 in production at Airbus Bremen (planned delivery 2027–2028). [ESA Artemis II page](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Orion/Artemis_II); [Airbus ESM press release](https://www.airbus.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-03-heart-of-the-mission-airbus-built-esm-to-power-historic-artemis-ii-crew-to-the-moon)

**CSA:**
- Jeremy Hansen is in orbit on his first spaceflight. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney issued a statement calling the launch *"a testament to Colonel Hansen's exceptional skill"* and described Canada as *"a nation of explorers, builders, and innovators."* Carney also spoke with U.S. President Donald Trump about the mission. [Canada.ca launch statement](https://www.canada.ca/en/space-agency/news/2026/03/happening-soon-launch-of-the-historic-artemis-ii-mission.html)
- CSA astronaut David Saint-Jacques hosted media at the John H. Chapman Space Centre in Longueuil on launch day; CSA astronaut Joshua Kutryk was on-site in Florida for media availability.
- CSA's future contribution: Canadarm3 (for the now-restructured surface-focused Artemis architecture). [CBC launch coverage](https://www.cbc.ca/news/science/livestory/artemis-ii-launch-nasa-live-coverage-canada-9.7149222)

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** Optimized Trajectory Correction Burn Placement for the NASA Artemis II Mission
  - **Authors:** David Woffinden, Randy Eckman, Shane Robinson (NASA JSC)
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20230000223 (AAS 23-062)](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)
  - **Relevance:** Directly applicable in-flight; describes the methodology for placing TCBs during the free-return mission accounting for GN&C performance, navigation uncertainty, crew schedule, and thruster selection. Mission controllers are executing these burns on Days 3–4.

- **Title:** NASA's SLS Super Heavy-Lift Rocket in Process for Artemis II and Beyond
  - **Authors:** NASA MSFC SLS Team
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20250011679 (December 22, 2025)](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250011679)
  - **Relevance:** Describes hardware production status for Artemis II through V; confirms successful RS-25 engine production certification in 2024 and EUS test article progress.

- **Title:** NASA's Space Launch System: Powering Artemis and Enabling Discovery
  - **Authors:** NASA SLS Program
  - **Source:** [NASA NTRS 20240014859 (November 20, 2024)](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20240014859)
  - **Relevance:** Post-Artemis I SLS performance data and lessons learned applied to Artemis II configuration.

- **Title:** MoonAnything: A Vision Benchmark with Large-Scale Lunar Supervised Data
  - **Authors:** Clémentine Grethen, Yuang Shi, Simone Gasparini, et al.
  - **Source:** [arXiv:2604.00682v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00682v1) (published days before Artemis II launch)
  - **Relevance:** Learning-based visual perception benchmark for lunar surface navigation; supports future Artemis surface missions and autonomous systems development.
