# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-02 05:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is approximately 6.5 hours into Flight Day 2 with all primary systems nominal and the crew preparing for tonight's Trans-Lunar Injection (TLI) burn, still on schedule for ~23:35 UTC. This report deepens coverage on three topics that received limited treatment in earlier reports: the heat shield safety controversy that surrounded the Flight Readiness Review, the science instrument suite including newly published details in *Nature*, and the legislative and contracting background behind NASA's SLS architecture pivot to ULA's Centaur V upper stage.

### Technical & Engineering

**Heat Shield Safety Controversy — Full Context:**
The most contentious pre-launch issue, now resolved for flight but worth documenting in full:

- Artemis I's Orion heat shield (Avcoat ablative material) charred unevenly during reentry in December 2022, losing material in patterns inconsistent with preflight models.
- For Artemis II, NASA's response was not to redesign the material or conduct another uncrewed test, but to:
  1. Redesign the shield tile blocks
  2. Switch from a skip reentry to a **direct (steeper) reentry trajectory**, which eliminates the second heating pulse implicated in uneven venting
- **Critical dissent:** Former NASA engineer and STS-107 (Columbia) board member **Charles Camarda** issued public statements — including to media outlets and in formal letters — saying he would not certify the vehicle as safe to fly. He cited parallels to the organizational dynamics preceding Challenger and Columbia, arguing that NASA's computational models were being accepted in lieu of empirical test data to reach a predetermined conclusion.
- A widely-read March 2026 essay at *Idle Words* titled ["Artemis II Is Not Safe to Fly"](https://idlewords.com/2026/03/artemis_ii_is_not_safe_to_fly.htm) laid out the dissenters' case in detail.
- NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman and Associate Administrator Amit Kshatriya publicly endorsed the flight; the Flight Readiness Review on March 12, 2026 was unanimous "go." Isaacman has stated that the direct reentry trajectory substantially changes the aerothermal environment and that analysis supports crew safety.
- No further information on in-flight recurrence of heat shield concerns; the issue will next be evaluated during reentry on ~April 11.

**SLS Architecture Pivot — Legislative and Contract Context:**
- In February 2026, NASA announced standardization to SLS Block 1 only, cancelling the Exploration Upper Stage (EUS), which had grown from a 2017 cost estimate of ~$962M to approximately **$2.8 billion** without a completed unit.
- On **March 6–7, 2026**, NASA awarded a sole-source contract to **United Launch Alliance (ULA)** for Centaur V upper stages to fly on Artemis IV and V, replacing the EUS. Centaur V currently flies as part of ULA's Vulcan Centaur rocket.
- The **2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act** (passed by Congress) allocated **$4.1 billion** for SLS rockets for Artemis IV and V, providing legislative backing despite the Trump administration's proposal to terminate SLS/Orion after Artemis III.
- [SpaceNews on Centaur V selection](https://spacenews.com/nasa-selects-centaur-for-new-sls-upper-stage/); [The Register](https://www.theregister.com/2026/03/09/nasa_centaur_v_sls/)

### Science & Instruments

*Nature* published a detailed feature on Artemis II science just before launch: ["Artemis II mission is about to fly humans to the Moon — here's the science"](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00964-4). Key additions to prior reports:

**AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):**
- Organ-on-a-chip devices roughly the size of a USB drive, containing bone marrow cells derived from pre-flight blood donations by each of the four crew members
- Measures how **deep-space radiation and microgravity combined** affect human tissue at the cellular level — first such device to fly beyond LEO
- Data will inform models of radiation-induced DNA damage rates in astronauts beyond Earth's magnetosphere

**ARCHeR Wristbands:**
- Wearable sensors monitoring sleep quality, stress biomarkers, cognitive performance metrics, and inter-crew teamwork dynamics in real time
- First deployment of this behavioral health monitoring system in deep space
- Data feeds Human Research Program teams at JSC in near-real time

**Radiation Suite:**
- Six **Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessors (HERA)** distributed throughout the crew module measure total ionizing dose and linear energy transfer spectra
- Each astronaut wears an individual **Crew Active Dosimeter** — first crewed mission to deploy these beyond LEO
- Data will be cross-referenced with the BioSentinel free-flyer results from Artemis I for biological dosimetry validation

**Lunar Flyby Science (Preparation, Not Yet Executed):**
- The Science Evaluation Room at JSC is staffed by experts in cratering, volcanism, tectonism, and lunar ice/volatiles
- Scientists will provide real-time guidance to the crew during the ~3-hour flyby window on April 6
- Approximately **21% of the lunar far side** will be sunlit at closest approach; crew will photograph with high-resolution cameras at angles impossible for orbital missions
- Solar corona observations planned as Orion passes through the lunar umbral shadow

**CubeSat Payloads — All Four Nominal:**
Status unchanged from 04:00 UTC report; all four in their deployed elliptical orbits and collecting data:
- ATENEA (CONAE, Argentina) — radiation shielding and GPS validation
- TACHELES (DLR, Germany) — space electronics radiation effects
- K-RadCube (KASA, South Korea) — human-tissue-equivalent biological dosimetry
- SHMS / Saudi HEO Magnetosphere Satellite (Saudi Space Agency) — space weather measurements

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** Artemis II mission is about to fly humans to the Moon — here's the science
  - **Authors:** Alexandra Witze (News Feature)
  - **Source:** [*Nature*, April 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00964-4)
  - **Relevance:** Best single-source overview of the science rationale; covers AVATAR, ARCHeR, radiation instruments, and lunar flyby geology objectives.

- **Title:** Orion Artemis II Optical Communications Architecture, Implementation and Test Systems
  - **Authors:** NASA JSC / Glenn Research Center
  - **Source:** [NTRS 20250010065](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250010065) — October 2025
  - **Relevance:** Detailed architecture of the O2O system (260 Mbps downlink, 20 Mbps uplink) currently under checkout — enables 4K video from the Moon.

- **Title:** Deep Space Radiation Measurements and Crew Radiation Protection for the NASA Artemis Program
  - **Authors:** NASA JSC / Langley Research Center
  - **Source:** [NTRS 20250002757](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250002757) — March 2025
  - **Relevance:** Crew radiation storm shelter protocols and dose rate benchmarks from Artemis I BioSentinel; informs real-time crew dosimetry interpretation during trans-lunar coast.

- **Title:** Optimized Trajectory Correction Burn Placement for the NASA Artemis II Mission
  - **Authors:** David Woffinden, Randy Eckman, Shane Robinson (NASA JSC)
  - **Source:** [NTRS 20230000223](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20230000223)
  - **Relevance:** TCB placement methodology applied post-TLI during 4-day outbound coast; mission controllers will execute these burns on Days 3–4.

- **Title:** Machine Learning-Based Radiation Forecasting for Deep Space Missions Using SDO Imagery
  - **Authors:** Gurav et al.
  - **Source:** [arXiv:2411.17703](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.17703) — November 2024
  - **Relevance:** Solar energetic particle forecasting for crew radiation risk; directly applicable during trans-lunar coast and lunar flyby.

- **Title:** Emerging Trends in Cislunar Space for Lunar Science Exploration and Space Robotics Aiding Human Spaceflight Safety
  - **Authors:** Arsalan Muhammad et al.
  - **Source:** [arXiv:2603.02878](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.02878) — March 2026
  - **Relevance:** Provides context for the restructured Artemis architecture (surface base replacing Gateway) and AI/robotics roles in future crewed lunar operations.
