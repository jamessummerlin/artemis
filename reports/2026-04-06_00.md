# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-06 00:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II is on Flight Day 6 — the mission's most significant day since launch. The crew entered the Moon's sphere of gravitational influence at approximately 12:41 a.m. EDT (4:41 AM UTC), the first humans in the Moon's gravity well since Apollo 17 in 1972. The lunar flyby observation window runs from 2:45–9:40 PM EDT today, culminating in closest approach at ~4,066 miles above the surface at 7:02 PM EDT, a new crewed distance record of 252,757 miles from Earth at 7:07 PM EDT, a planned ~40-minute communications blackout as Orion passes behind the Moon, and a rare ~1-hour solar eclipse beginning at approximately 8:35 PM EDT.

## Key Updates

### Official NASA Updates

- **Flight Day 6 Flyby Window (April 6, 2026):** Orion's main cabin windows are oriented toward the Moon from 2:45–9:40 PM EDT, providing the crew a continuous visual observation and photography session covering the lunar nearside, south and north poles, and far-side regions never previously seen directly by humans.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/missions/artemis/artemis-2/nasa-sets-coverage-for-artemis-ii-moon-mission/

- **Lunar Sphere of Influence:** Crew entered the Moon's gravitational sphere of influence at approximately 12:41 AM EDT — the first humans in the Moon's gravity well since Apollo 17 on December 14, 1972.

- **Distance Record:** Orion is expected to surpass the Apollo 13 crewed distance record of ~248,655 miles at 1:56 PM EDT, reaching a maximum of **252,757 miles (406,773 km)** from Earth at 7:07 PM EDT.
  - Source: https://www.nbcmiami.com/news/national-international/artemis-ii-break-apollo-13-distance-record-moon-flyby/3791209/

- **Communications Blackout:** When Orion passes behind the Moon at approximately 5:47 PM EDT, a planned ~40-minute communications blackout begins. The Moon blocks signals between the Deep Space Network and Orion. Crew will continue recording observations and commentary for post-reacquisition downlink.

- **Earthrise:** Earth comes back into view from Orion's perspective at ~7:25 PM EDT as it emerges from behind the opposite lunar limb; Mission Control is expected to re-acquire communications at the same time. NASA plans to capture a modern sequel to the iconic Apollo 8 "Earthrise" photograph.

- **Solar Eclipse:** Beginning at approximately 8:35 PM EDT (0035 GMT April 7), the Sun will disappear behind the Moon from Orion's perspective for approximately one hour — one of the rarest skywatching phenomena possible. The crew will observe the solar corona visible at the lunar limb, search for meteoroid impact flashes on the dark lunar surface, and look for dust lofting above the limb.
  - Source: https://www.space.com/space-exploration/artemis/artemis-2-astronauts-are-about-to-see-one-of-the-rarest-skywatching-sights-of-all-a-solar-eclipse-from-beyond-the-moon

- **Live coverage** aired beginning 1:00 PM EDT on NASA+, Amazon Prime, Apple TV, Hulu, Netflix, HBO Max, and Roku.
  - Source: https://plus.nasa.gov/scheduled-video/nasas-artemis-ii-crew-flies-around-the-moon-official-broadcast/

- **Post-flyby plan:** After the flyby window ends at 9:40 PM EDT, the crew will begin transferring imagery to the ground. NASA's science team will review images and observations overnight and debrief with the crew on April 7. No mission status briefing was held today due to flyby activities; briefings resume April 7.

### Technical & Engineering

**Orion ("Integrity") — Flyby Configuration**
- Final pre-flyby trajectory correction burn executed successfully ahead of the observation window, confirming closest approach parameters of ~4,066 miles.
- All four solar array wings remain deployed and nominal.
- Orion's windows oriented toward the Moon for the duration of the 2:45–9:40 PM EDT observation window.
- Crew configured handheld cameras with 80–400 mm and 14–24 mm lenses for photography.
- Following the flyby, Orion begins its return trajectory to Earth, targeting April 10 splashdown.

**Communications Blackout — Deep Space Network (DSN)**
- The ~40-minute blackout beginning ~5:47 PM EDT is a planned and expected consequence of Orion passing behind the Moon. No anomaly is implied.
- Crew will continue recording video and commentary during blackout for downlink after signal reacquisition at ~7:25 PM EDT.

**O2O Optical Communications**
- The Orion Artemis II Optical Communications laser payload continues nominal operation at up to 260 Mbps downlink, providing high-bandwidth data relay throughout the flyby.
  - Source: https://www.nasa.gov/technology/space-comms/o2o/

**Heat Shield**
- No new in-flight heat shield data. Post-splashdown inspection on April 10 will validate the steeper direct-reentry profile designed after Artemis I's ablative anomalies.

### Science & Instruments

- **Lunar Observation Campaign:** The crew is executing the 30-target Lunar Targeting Plan (LTP) during the flyby. The observation list was finalized and transmitted April 5. Key targets:
  - **Orientale Basin:** ~600-mile-wide impact crater straddling the near/far-side boundary, 3.8 billion years old; fully illuminated as Orion approaches. First direct human observations of this feature.
  - **Hertzsprung Crater:** Geological comparison with Orientale to study crater evolution.
  - Far-side regions — including areas entirely inaccessible from Earth-based telescopes — represent a significant portion of the 30 targets.
  - Source: https://wtop.com/the-space-place/2026/04/heres-how-artemis-astronauts-will-document-parts-of-the-moon-never-seen-before/
  - Source: https://spacenews.com/nasa-finalizes-science-plans-for-artemis-2-lunar-flyby/

- **Solar Eclipse Science:** During the ~1-hour solar eclipse, the crew will:
  - Analyze the solar corona visible at the lunar limb
  - Search for meteoroid impact flashes on the dark lunar surface (hazard assessment for future landed missions)
  - Observe potential dust lofting above the lunar limb
  - Observe deep-space targets (planets, stars) with the Sun blocked

- **AVATAR Investigation:** Organ-on-a-chip radiation/microgravity biology experiment — active throughout the mission.
- **ARCHeR wristband:** Continuous crew biometric and radiation health monitoring in deep space.
- **CubeSats (K-RadCube, Space Weather CubeSat-1, TACHELES, ATENEA):** All four confirmed operational in their independent orbits as of last status.

### Crew Updates

- All four crew members are in good health entering the lunar flyby.
- **Commander Reid Wiseman (NASA):** Leading the flyby observation session. Oldest person to travel beyond low Earth orbit.
- **Pilot Victor Glover (NASA):** First person of color to travel beyond LEO.
- **Mission Specialist 1 Christina Koch (NASA):** First woman to travel beyond LEO. Described seeing the far side of the Moon from Orion: *"The darker parts just aren't quite in the right place. And something about you senses that is not the moon that I'm used to seeing."* The crew used study materials in real-time to correlate what they were seeing with known lunar geography.
  - Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/science/space/artemis-ii-astronauts-interview-space-moon-far-side-nasa-rcna266564
- **Mission Specialist 2 Jeremy Hansen (CSA):** First Canadian and first non-American to travel beyond LEO. On the experience: *"Right away, you are humbled. The fact that four of us get to be out here just brings you to your knees."* On the photos beamed back: *"I know those photos are amazing, but let me assure you, it is another level of amazing up here."*
  - Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/science/space/artemis-ii-astronauts-interview-space-moon-far-side-nasa-rcna266564
  - Source: https://www.cbc.ca/news/science/artemis-jeremy-hansen-9.7153552
- Jeremy Hansen is carrying the Innu Nation flag aboard Orion, honoring an Indigenous partnership from his home country.
  - Source: https://vocm.com/2026/04/03/innu-nation-hansen-flag/
