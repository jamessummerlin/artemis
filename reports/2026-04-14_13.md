# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-14 13:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II remains in the post-mission phase with all four crew members undergoing reconditioning and science debriefs at Johnson Space Center ahead of the April 16 postflight news conference. The most notable new development since the 06:00 UTC report is NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman publicly clarifying social-media speculation about an apparent "missing chunk" on Orion's heat shield: he confirmed the discoloration is a normal ablation byproduct and no material was liberated. The 30-day formal heat-shield inspection is ongoing, with the capsule en route to Kennedy Space Center for detailed scanning.

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Post-mission — crew reconditioning, engineering data analysis, science debriefs at JSC
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35 UTC, Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown:** April 10, 2026 at ~00:07 UTC (April 9, 5:07 p.m. PDT), Pacific Ocean ~50 miles off San Diego; recovered by USS John P. Murtha (LPD-26)
- **Mission Duration:** ~9 days, 1 hour, 31 minutes
- **Notable Changes:**
  - No new mission-profile changes since 06:00 UTC
  - Heat shield: NASA formally rebutted "missing chunk" media speculation; 30-day formal inspection period underway; capsule being transported to KSC
  - Postflight news conference confirmed for **April 16, 2026 at 2:30 p.m. EDT** at JSC

### Technical & Engineering

- **Heat shield "missing chunk" clarification (NEW since 06:00 UTC):** After a zoomed-in post-splashdown photo prompted social-media speculation about liberated material on the Orion heat shield, NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman directly addressed the concern:
  > *"The discoloration was not liberated material. The white color observed corresponds to the compression pad area and is consistent with the local geometry, AVCOAT byproducts, and transitional heating environments."*
  - Orion program manager Howard Hu confirmed no unexpected conditions were found in initial diver imagery or aboard-ship inspection.
  - A formal **30-day inspection period** is underway; the capsule will be transported to Kennedy Space Center for detailed scans of the Avcoat ablative layer.
  - [NASA Sets the Record Straight on That 'Missing Chunk' of Artemis 2's Heat Shield — Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/nasa-administrator-sets-the-record-straight-on-that-missing-chunk-of-artemis-2s-heat-shield-2000745676)
  - [Heat Shield Data from Artemis II Could Define Timeline of Future Lunar Missions — ABC15](https://www.abc15.com/science-and-tech/space/heat-shield-data-from-artemis-ii-could-define-artemis-iv-timeline)
  - [NASA Shares Orion Heat Shield Findings, Updates Artemis Moon Missions — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-shares-orion-heat-shield-findings-updates-artemis-moon-missions/)
- **Re-entry summary:** Orion used a modified "loft" (non-skip) re-entry profile to limit Avcoat heating and cracking — the primary mitigation developed after the Artemis I heat-shield anomaly. Entry, descent, and landing systems performed as designed. Peak re-entry speed ~25,000 mph (40,000 km/h).
- **SLS/Orion primary objectives confirmed met:**
  - Life support systems: all verified nominal with crew aboard in deep space
  - Manual piloting tests: Koch/Hansen (FD4), Wiseman/Glover (FD8–9)
  - Perigee Raise Burn (FD2), TLI, four course correction burns (TCB-Return-1 through -3), deorbit burn — all executed as planned
  - ESM: European Service Module operated nominally throughout; post-flight performance data being analyzed by Airbus/ESA
- **Crew recovery in detail:** U.S. Navy dive teams from the USS John P. Murtha provided first medical contact. A Navy/Fleet Forces story details the recovery team's role.
  - [First Contact: Meet the Dive Medical Recovery Team of Artemis II — U.S. Fleet Forces Command](https://www.usff.navy.mil/Press-Room/News-Stories/Article/4456440/first-contact-meet-the-dive-medical-recovery-team-of-artemis-ii/)

### Science & Instruments

- **7,000+ lunar flyby images (April 6):** Released to the public; include Earthset and Earthrise from the lunar far side, Orientale Basin views, Vavilov Crater, ancient lava flows, color variations, meteoroid impact flashes (six observed), and views of the solar eclipse (54-min totality) and the Milky Way.
  - [Artemis II Crew Beams Official Moon Flyby Photos to Earth — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasas-artemis-ii-crew-beams-official-moon-flyby-photos-to-earth/)
  - [Artemis II Lunar Flyby Gallery — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/gallery/lunar-flyby/)
- **Science debriefs underway at JSC:** Crew have joined science teams for in-depth debriefs on Crew Lunar Observations (CLO), AVATAR human tissue investigation, and other human research performance studies. Data processing continues.
- **Geological reconnaissance:** Images along the lunar terminator near the planned South Pole landing zone for Artemis IV (2028) are being analyzed for lighting and terrain characterization.
- **CubeSats deployed:** Four 12U international CubeSats (TACHELES/Germany, K-Rad Cube/South Korea, SHMS/Saudi Arabia, Argentina CubeSat) deployed in high Earth orbit; teams now receiving data from their respective payloads.
- **AVATAR investigation:** Bio-sample collection complete; deep space radiation + microgravity response data from crew being processed at JSC.
- **Crater naming — Carroll & Integrity:** Formal IAU submissions underway; ruling expected within ~1 month.
  - [Artemis II Names Moon Crater Carroll After Reid Wiseman's Late Wife — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/07/nx-s1-5776440/artemis-moon-crater-carroll-wiseman)

### Crew Updates
