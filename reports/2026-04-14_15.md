# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-14 15:00 UTC

## Summary

The primary new development this cycle is expanded scientific reporting on the six meteoroid impact flashes observed by the crew during the April 6 lunar flyby — a serendipitous discovery that drew "audible screams of delight" from NASA scientists and will be cross-referenced with lunar orbital satellite data. The crew continues postflight reconditioning (Day 4 since splashdown) at JSC, with the April 16 postflight news conference confirmed and media credentialing now closed (5 p.m. CDT deadline passed). No major new engineering or programmatic developments beyond those already documented in the 14:00 UTC report.

### Technical & Engineering

- **Heat shield loft-reentry profile — engineering detail:** Detailed reporting confirms that NASA modified the reentry trajectory from a skip to a loft profile — coming in at a steeper angle and spending less time in the portion of the atmosphere where Artemis I experienced AVCOAT char-layer cracking and fragmentation. Engineers at JSC are comparing post-flight heat shield performance against this modified profile; no public anomaly statements issued. Further findings expected in upcoming weeks as the thermal protection system is inspected.

### Science & Instruments

- **Meteoroid impact flashes — unexpected science highlight:** This cycle's most significant new science story. During the seven-hour lunar flyby on April 6, the Artemis II crew observed **six brief white-to-bluish-white flashes** on the darkened lunar surface — direct visual confirmation of meteoroid impacts on the Moon in real time. The majority of flashes were seen during the solar eclipse when the Moon's nearside was darkened, improving contrast. Lunar science lead **Kelsey Young** (NASA) reported "audible screams of delight" from ground scientists when the crew reported the sightings. Each flash lasted approximately one millisecond. NASA scientists are now working to cross-reference the crew's visual observations with data from a lunar orbital satellite (likely LRO) to characterize the impactors. The GEODES (Geophysical Exploration of the Dynamics and Evolution of the Solar System) program at the University of Maryland operates an impact-flash database and may be involved in follow-up analysis. This opportunistic observation highlights the scientific value of crewed deep-space presence and will inform planning for future lunar base monitoring of meteoroid flux.

The CLO team continues processing 10,000+ images.

- **Day 4 postflight reconditioning:** Crew is in the fourth day of standard NASA postflight protocols: medical evaluations (cardiovascular, vestibular, musculoskeletal), human performance evaluations, and debriefs with mission science teams. A 10-day mission like Artemis II typically involves 21–45 days of reconditioning, with most crew members expected to return to light duties within 2–3 weeks.

- **Carroll and Integrity crater names — broader coverage:** The crater naming proposals have received significant international media coverage. "Carroll" is a ~3-mile-wide shallow crater at the lunar limb, on the boundary between near and far sides, and is occasionally visible from Earth. "Integrity" (named after the Orion capsule) is slightly larger and fully on the far side. Both names were proposed during the April 6 flyby following the tradition of Apollo crews naming features after loved ones or their spacecraft. IAU ruling anticipated around May 2026.
  - [Moon memorial: Artemis 2 astronauts name lunar 'bright spot' after commander's late wife — Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/artemis/moon-memorial-artemis-2-astronauts-name-lunar-bright-spot-after-mission-commanders-late-wife)
