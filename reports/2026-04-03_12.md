# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-03

## Summary
Artemis II launched successfully on April 1, 2026 at 6:35 p.m. EDT from Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39B, sending four astronauts on a historic ~10-day crewed lunar flyby — the first since Apollo. As of April 3, the crew is en route to the Moon following a successful Translunar Injection (TLI) burn on April 2, with lunar closest approach expected around April 6 and splashdown in the Pacific Ocean targeted for April 10.

## Mission Status
- **Current Phase:** In-flight — translunar transit, Day 2/3 post-launch
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC (6:35 p.m. EDT)
- **Splashdown Target:** ~April 10, 2026, Pacific Ocean
- **Notable Changes:** Mission experienced multiple delays from the original February 5, 2026 target window: a liquid hydrogen leak during wet dress rehearsal pushed launch to March, then a helium flow issue triggered a rollback to the VAB and delayed to April. Final launch proceeded April 1 with only minor battery and range issues.

### Technical & Engineering

**SLS Rocket**
- SLS Block 1 rocket with twin solid rocket boosters generated 8.8 million pounds of thrust at liftoff; the solid rocket boosters alone provided more than 75% of initial thrust.
- Rocket mass at launch: approximately 5.75 million pounds.
- Core stage separation and ICPS (Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage) separation both completed nominally.
- Rollout from the Vehicle Assembly Building to Launch Complex 39B occurred January 18, 2026.

**Orion Spacecraft (named "Integrity")**
- All four solar array wings deployed and locked into place after launch; power generation nominal.
- Proximity operations demonstration: Over ~70 minutes, Pilot Victor Glover manually maneuvered Orion through controlled approach and retreat patterns using the ICPS as a reference target. Performance exceeded simulator predictions.
  - Source: https://starlust.org/artemis-ii-completes-proximity-operations-demonstration-orions-perigee-raise-burn-to-follow/
- TLI burn: ~5 minutes 50 seconds main engine firing on April 2 placed Orion on a free-return lunar trajectory.
- Minor toilet anomaly resolved in flight by crew and ground teams.

**Heat Shield**
- Significant pre-launch controversy: Post-flight inspection of Artemis I (2022) found 100+ locations of irregular char loss from the Avcoat heat shield. Root cause was identified as insufficient porosity allowing gas pressure buildup and cracking during reentry.
- NASA's mitigations for Artemis II:
  1. Redesigned heat shield blocks with improved porosity.
  2. Switched to direct reentry trajectory with a steeper descent angle to reduce time in peak thermal environment.
- Flight Readiness Review on March 12, 2026 produced a unanimous "Go" vote from mission managers; NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman supported proceeding.
- Some dissenting voices: Former NASA astronaut and heat shield expert Charles Camarda publicly stated the shield is a "deviant" system and that NASA cannot reliably predict failure modes. Some engineers objected during the March 12 review.
- Two former astronauts met with the review team in January 2026 to discuss concerns; afterward they appeared to accept the decision to proceed.
  - Sources: https://phys.org/news/2026-03-shield-safety-stakes-nasa-artemis.html | https://theconversation.com/heat-shield-safety-concerns-raise-stakes-for-nasas-artemis-ii-moon-mission-275853

**Trajectory**
- Mission uses a free-return trajectory (no lunar orbit insertion), minimizing fuel usage and risk.
- Profile: Launch → high Earth orbit (~24-hour period) → proximity operations demo → perigee raise burn → TLI burn → ~4-day transit to Moon → lunar flyby (~4,000–6,000 miles closest approach) → free-return transit back to Earth → direct reentry → Pacific splashdown.
- Lunar closest approach and farthest-distance-from-Earth record attempt (~252,000 miles, exceeding Apollo 13's ~248,655 miles) targeted for April 6 at ~1:45 p.m. EDT.
  - Source: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/4/2/how-long-will-artemis-ii-take-to-reach-the-moon-and-what-happens-next

**Ground Systems**
- Launch Complex 39B (344-acre site, 60 launches since Apollo 10 in 1969) was the launch site.
- Rocco Petrone Launch Control Center hosted launch team console operations.
- Exploration Ground Systems (EGS) at Kennedy Space Center managed processing, launch, and recovery systems.
- A North American winter storm in January 2026 briefly delayed pre-launch preparations.
  - Source: https://www.kennedyspacecenter.com/event/nasa-space-launch-system-sls-artemis-ii/

### Science & Instruments

**Crew Health Experiments**
- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response):** Uses organ-on-a-chip devices to study effects of radiation and microgravity on human health. Blood and saliva samples will measure immune system changes.
- **ARCHeR:** A watch-like wearable device monitoring crew well-being, activity levels, and sleep patterns throughout the mission.
  - Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00964-4

**Lunar Surface Observations**
- Crew will photograph lunar far side during ~April 6 flyby. The partial illumination at flyby geometry will create oblique shadows enhancing surface relief, revealing crater rims, ridges, and slopes difficult to detect under full illumination.
- Photos of impact craters and ancient lava flows could provide new data on the Moon's geological history.

**CubeSats (deployed ~5 hours after launch from the Orion stage adapter)**
- **K-RadCube (South Korea — Korea AeroSpace Administration / Korea Astronomy and Space Science Institute):** Measuring space radiation levels across the Van Allen Radiation Belts. Confirmed operational after deployment.
  - Source: https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/04/02/satellite-cubesat/5711775174421/
- **Space Weather CubeSat-1 (Saudi Arabia — Saudi Space Agency):** Measuring space weather parameters including radiation, solar X-rays, solar energetic particles, and magnetic fields.
- **TACHELES (Germany — DLR, German Aerospace Center):** Demonstrating in-space technologies including electrical components for future lunar logistics vehicles.
  - Source: https://www.dlr.de/en/latest/news/2026/artemis-ii-to-launch-for-the-moon-with-german-and-european-tech-on-board
- **ATENEA (Argentina — CONAE, National Space Activities Commission):** Investigating radiation shielding, orbital design optimization, and long-range communications.
  - Source: https://digitalcommons.usu.edu/smallsat/2025/E-S2-2025/1/

All four CubeSats were deployed after SLS stage adapter separation. These international science payloads will expand knowledge of the deep space radiation and electromagnetic environment at high Earth orbit and beyond.

**Full science overview:** https://science.nasa.gov/artemis-ii-science/

### Crew Updates
- **Commander Reid Wiseman (NASA):** Leading the mission; cleared for flight.
- **Pilot Victor Glover (NASA):** Manually flew proximity operations demonstration; reported vehicle real-world performance exceeded simulator; first Black astronaut to travel to the Moon vicinity.
- **Mission Specialist Christina Koch (NASA):** First woman to travel to the vicinity of the Moon.
- **Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA — Canadian Space Agency):** First non-American to travel beyond low Earth orbit; first Canadian to travel to the Moon vicinity.
- Crew was photographed playing cards during pre-launch suit-up day (April 1) before heading to the launch pad.

## Upcoming Milestones
- **~April 6, 2026 (~1:45 p.m. EDT):** Orion passes ~252,000 miles from Earth, breaking Apollo 13's distance record; lunar closest approach (~4,000–6,000 miles from lunar surface); crew photographs lunar far side.
- **~April 10, 2026:** Planned splashdown in the Pacific Ocean; U.S. Navy recovery operations.
- **Post-mission:** Post-flight inspection of heat shield will be critical data point for Artemis III planning.
- **Mid-2027:** NASA plans to launch one or both Human Landing System (HLS) vehicles to low Earth orbit for Artemis III rendezvous and docking tests.
- **~2027:** Artemis III crewed lunar landing mission (NET 2027).
- **Cancelled:** Lunar Gateway space station program cancelled by NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman in March 2026. Gateway components are being redirected toward plans for a permanent lunar surface base.
