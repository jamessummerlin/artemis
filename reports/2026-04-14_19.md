# Artemis II Mission Update — 2026-04-14 19:00 UTC

## Summary

Artemis II completed its historic 10-day crewed lunar flyby on April 10, 2026, with a successful splashdown at 5:07 p.m. PDT (00:07 UTC April 11) off the coast of San Diego; all four crew members are healthy and undergoing post-flight rehabilitation at Johnson Space Center. Initial heat shield inspection following splashdown found no unexpected conditions, though a 30-day detailed analysis at Kennedy Space Center is ongoing after social media attention to apparent ablation in the compression pad area. A major program-level shift has also emerged: NASA officially cancelled the Lunar Gateway on March 24 and is redirecting $20 billion toward a phased lunar surface base, with Artemis IV now the first crewed landing target (~2028).

---

## Mission Status

- **Current Phase:** Post-mission — crew in post-flight rehabilitation, evaluations, and lunar science debriefs at JSC; mission fully complete
- **Launch Date:** April 1, 2026 at 22:35:12 UTC, Launch Complex 39B, Kennedy Space Center
- **Splashdown:** April 10, 2026 at 5:07 p.m. PDT / 8:07 p.m. EDT (April 11, 00:07 UTC), Pacific Ocean ~50–80 miles off San Diego; recovery vessel USS John P. Murtha (LPD 26)
- **Mission Duration:** Approximately 9 days, 25.5 hours (~218 hours)
- **Total Distance Traveled:** 694,481 miles — new human spaceflight distance record (surpassing Apollo 13's 1970 record)
- **Farthest Distance from Earth:** 252,756 miles during lunar flyby (April 6)
- **Notable Changes Since Last Report (April 8, 17:00 UTC):**
  - Mission concluded successfully; no anomalies affected crew safety
  - Heat shield "missing chunk" social media story emerged and was addressed by NASA
  - Lunar Gateway officially cancelled; Artemis program architecture restructured
  - Crew postflight news conference scheduled for April 16 at JSC

### Technical & Engineering

- **Heat shield — "missing chunk" social media story and NASA response:**
  After splashdown, a zoomed-in photograph circulating on social media appeared to show a large piece missing from Orion's heat shield. NASA responded that the discoloration and apparent gap is consistent with the compression pad area and represents expected AVCOAT byproducts and transitional heating — not liberated material. This was confirmed in arc jet testing prior to flight.
  - [NASA Sets the Record Straight on That 'Missing Chunk' of Artemis 2's Heat Shield — Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/nasa-administrator-sets-the-record-straight-on-that-missing-chunk-of-artemis-2s-heat-shield-2000745676)
  - [NASA responds after social media users notice apparent missing part on Artemis 2's heat shield — UNILAD Tech](https://www.uniladtech.com/science/space/nasa-responds-missing-artemis-2-heat-shield-546085-20260414)

- **Heat shield initial post-splashdown inspection:**
  Divers photographed the heat shield immediately after splashdown while Orion remained in the Pacific. Two heat shield engineers aboard the USS John P. Murtha inspected the capsule upon recovery. Initial findings: no unexpected conditions observed. A 30-day detailed inspection period will follow, culminating in a report. Orion is being transported to Kennedy Space Center for detailed material scans.
  - [NASA confident Artemis 2 heat shield will protect crew during re-entry — Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/04/10/nasa-confident-artemis-ii-heat-shield-will-protect-crew-during-re-entry/)
  - [NASA Shares Orion Heat Shield Findings, Updates Artemis Moon Missions — NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-shares-orion-heat-shield-findings-updates-artemis-moon-missions/)

- **Heat shield data and implications for Artemis IV:**
  Data from Artemis II re-entry instrumentation is considered critical for finalizing the modified re-entry trajectory approach adopted for crewed lunar-return missions after the Artemis I ablation anomaly. Analysis will influence the Orion heat shield manufacturing improvements already being implemented for Artemis III and Artemis IV.
  - [Heat shield data from Artemis II could define Artemis IV timeline — NBC26](https://www.nbc26.com/science-and-tech/space/heat-shield-data-from-artemis-ii-could-define-artemis-iv-timeline)

- **Re-entry performance:**
  Orion entered Earth's atmosphere at approximately 24,500 mph and slowed to ~325 mph before parachute deployment. Temperatures at the heat shield reached approximately 3,000°F (vs. ~5,000°F for a direct high-speed return). The modified skip-entry trajectory used a shallower angle, as adopted after the Artemis I heat shield investigation.

- **Naval recovery — USS John P. Murtha (LPD 26):**
  Detailed account of recovery operations published by USNI News.
  - ['Integrity, Arriving': Navy Crew Recovers Astronauts After Artemis Mission — USNI News](https://news.usni.org/2026/04/12/integrity-arriving)

### Science & Instruments

- **Crew Lunar Observations (CLO) — preliminary highlights:**
  The crew captured more than 7,000 images during the April 6 lunar flyby, including:
  - Earthrise and Earthset sequences from cislunar space
  - Imagery of the Vavilov Crater (on the rim of Hertzsprung Basin, lunar farside) — captured by Koch and Glover using 400mm handheld cameras
  - Solar corona imagery during the lunar eclipse of the Sun as viewed from Orion
  - Terminator-zone terrain imagery under low-angle solar illumination (relevant to south polar landing zone topography for Artemis IV)
  - Flash monitoring for meteoroid impacts on the lunar surface
  - [Artemis II Science Data 2026: First Results from Vavilov Crater & Radiation Tests — College Simplified](https://www.collegesimplified.in/post/artemis-ii-science-data-downlink-flight-day-7-2026)
  - [The best images from Artemis II — The Planetary Society](https://www.planetary.org/articles/the-best-images-from-artemis-ii)

- **AVATAR (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response) — samples recovered:**
  The organ-on-a-chip devices containing crew-derived cells have been recovered from Orion and are being analyzed at the laboratory. Data will quantify how the combination of deep-space radiation and microgravity affects human tissue at the cellular level. Results will be published in future reports.
  - [NASA Artemis II Science | AVATAR — NASA Science](https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/investigations/avatar/)
  - [Why NASA sent 'organ chips' of the Artemis II crew into space — CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/09/science/artemis-2-human-health-experiments)

- **Radiation dosimetry — preliminary findings:**
  Preliminary radiation dosimetry data from personal dosimeters and Orion's internal detectors:
  - Each crew member accumulated approximately **12 millisieverts (mSv)** over the 10-day mission
  - This is approximately **18% above pre-flight model predictions**
  - All exposures remain well within NASA's career radiation limits
  - Blood samples taken during flight are being analyzed at JSC for chromosomal biomarkers (double-strand DNA breaks in white blood cells)
  - Long-term monitoring under the Lifetime Surveillance of Astronaut Health (LSAH) program will continue for years
  - [Artemis II Crew Heading Home: Space Radiation Exposure Data Raises Questions — Health Wire USA](https://healthwireusa.com/artemis-ii-crew-space-radiation-exposure-data-questions/)
  - [Deep Space Radiation Measurements and Crew Radiation Protection — NTRS 20250002757](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250002757)

- **ARCHeR (Artemis Research for Crew Health and Readiness):**
  A new NTRS entry (20260002430) has appeared documenting the ARCHeR investigation update as of Artemis II. ARCHeR uses machine learning and behavioral coding of audiovisual data to monitor crew health and performance unobtrusively. Post-mission analysis of Artemis II ARCHeR data is underway.
  - [PROJECT ARCHeR: Artemis II Update — NTRS 20260002430](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260002430)

### International Partners

- **CSA (Canada):**
  - The Carney/Hansen call on April 8 (23:20 UTC) proceeded as scheduled. It was the final planned space-to-Earth crew connection of the Artemis II mission, hosted by CSA astronaut Joshua Kutryk at the John H. Chapman Space Centre.
  - Post-mission, CSA has published commemorative content and briefings noting Hansen as the first non-U.S. citizen to reach the lunar vicinity in more than 50 years.
  - [Artemis II astronauts splash down — CBC coverage](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/artemis-ii-splashdown-return/)

- **ESA (Europe):**
  - The ESA-built European Service Module (ESM-2) performed nominally throughout the mission and separated from the Crew Module prior to re-entry as designed. ESA has released post-mission statements celebrating the ESM's performance.
  - [ESA — Artemis II](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Orion/Artemis_II)

- **Program-level — Gateway cancellation impact on partners:**
  NASA's March 24 decision to cancel the Lunar Gateway left international partners (ESA, JAXA, CSA, MDA) in an uncertain position regarding their Gateway contributions (HALO, I-HAB, refueling element, Canadarm3). Bloomberg reported that partners were caught off-guard. No resolution has been formally announced as of April 14.
  - [NASA's Pause on Gateway Lunar Orbiter Leaves Allies in Lurch — Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-01/nasa-s-pause-on-gateway-lunar-orbiter-leaves-allies-in-lurch)

## Academic & Research Papers

- **Title:** PROJECT ARCHeR: Artemis II Update (NTRS 20260002430)
  - **Authors:** NASA Human Research Program team
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server; acquired April 2026
  - **Relevance:** Documents the deployable framework for ARCHeR (Artemis Research for Crew Health and Readiness), which uses machine learning and behavioral coding of audiovisual data collected during Artemis II to assess crew health and performance. This is the first post-flight NTRS document specific to Artemis II science outcomes.
  - **URL:** [NTRS 20260002430](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20260002430)

- **Title:** Deep Space Radiation Measurements and Crew Radiation Protection for the NASA Artemis Program (NTRS 20250002757)
  - **Authors:** NASA JSC / Space Radiation Laboratory team
  - **Source:** NASA Technical Reports Server
  - **Relevance:** Foundational document for the radiation dosimetry framework and models used on Artemis II; the 18% exceedance over pre-flight predictions now being analyzed against this document's model predictions.
  - **URL:** [NTRS 20250002757](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250002757)

- **Title:** Artemis II Science Data 2026: First Results from Vavilov Crater & Radiation Tests
  - **Authors:** NASA lunar science team / CLO investigation team
  - **Source:** NASA / reported via multiple outlets; primary results pending peer-reviewed publication
  - **Relevance:** First publicly available preliminary CLO image analysis, highlighting Vavilov Crater (farside) and solar corona observations as scientifically notable from the flyby dataset.
